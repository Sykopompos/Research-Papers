# The Wrong Test at the Wrong Time

## A Mechanistic Framework for Non-IgE and Delayed Hypersensitivity Diagnosis

*Allergy Testing, ILC2-Mediated Barrier Failure, Delay Kinetics, and a Rational Diagnostic Architecture for EoE, FPIES, and Type III/IV Reactions*

## Abstract

Allergy diagnosis is built almost entirely around IgE-mediated immediate hypersensitivity. The diagnostic tools for this mechanism — skin prick testing, serum specific IgE, and basophil activation testing — are accurate and increasingly well-validated for what they measure. The problem is what they do not measure. A large and clinically important class of immune reactions operates through entirely different mechanisms: non-IgE barrier-driven Th2 cascades, T cell-mediated delayed hypersensitivity, and immune complex deposition. For most of these reactions, IgE testing is not merely imprecise — it is structurally the wrong instrument for the biology involved. The diagnostic failure is systematic and predictable, not accidental.

This paper proposes a mechanistic framework for understanding and diagnosing delayed and non-IgE hypersensitivity. The unifying structure is the same across conditions: epithelial barrier disruption triggers innate alarm cytokines (TSLP, IL-33, IL-25), which activate group 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s) and polarise dendritic cells toward Th2. The resulting cytokine cascade — IL-5, IL-13, eotaxin-3 for eosinophilic reactions; IFN-γ for Th1/Type IV T cell reactions — then accumulates in target tissue over hours to days before crossing the symptom threshold. The delay is not mysterious: it is the kinetics of cellular recruitment, not a threshold-crossing event.

The framework is applied to eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE), food protein-induced enterocolitis syndrome (FPIES), delayed drug reactions (DRESS, SJS), and hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP). For each, we identify the precise mechanism, the correct diagnostic tool, and why standard IgE testing fails. We also address neurochemical and hormonal amplification layers — substance P, VIP depletion, and estradiol mast cell priming — as mechanistic proposals supported by translational evidence but not yet directly quantified in non-IgE food reactions. Seven testable predictions are derived. This paper is a mechanistic synthesis and hypothesis-generating narrative review. No new experimental data are reported.

**Keywords:** delayed hypersensitivity; non-IgE food allergy; eosinophilic esophagitis; FPIES; basophil activation test; component-resolved diagnostics; ILC2; TSLP; IL-33; eotaxin-3; substance P; VIP; estradiol; mast cell; Th2

## Methods and Scope

This paper presents a mechanistic framework for non-IgE and delayed hypersensitivity, drawing on established immunology, epithelial biology, neuroimmunology, and recent clinical and diagnostic evidence. Literature was identified through targeted searches of PubMed and Google Scholar. Studies were weighted according to design: clinical RCTs and prospective cohort studies form the evidentiary backbone for diagnostic tool performance claims; mechanistic evidence is drawn from replicated human translational studies; case series, workshop reports, and animal model data are used only for hypothesis framing and are labelled as such. Evidence quality is noted where relevant: established mechanisms with replicated human evidence are stated as such; mechanistically supported but not fully clinically validated claims are explicitly framed as proposed or consistent with mechanism; and integrative inferences are framed as hypotheses. Approximately 75 primary and review sources were considered; 27 are cited. No new experimental data are reported.

## §1 — The Diagnostic Mismatch: Why the Wrong Test Is Standard of Care

When a patient reports that food makes them ill, the standard clinical response is to order a skin prick test and a serum IgE panel. This is appropriate medicine for one specific immune mechanism. It is the wrong test for a large and growing class of immune reactions, and applying it universally has produced a diagnostic system that predictably underserves patients with eosinophilic esophagitis, FPIES, and most delayed food reactions — for whom IgE testing is not just insensitive but structurally irrelevant [Furuta07, Nowak-Wegrzyn17].

The mismatch is not a failure of individual clinicians. It is a structural consequence of diagnostic tool availability. The problem is that clinical practice has generalised 'allergy testing' to mean 'IgE testing', then concluded 'no allergy' when IgE tests are negative in patients with clearly immune-mediated disease. For EoE, the average diagnostic delay is 5–7 years [Furuta07]. For FPIES, it is frequently misdiagnosed as viral gastroenteritis or dismissed as non-organic [Nowak-Wegrzyn17]. For Type IV drug reactions like DRESS and SJS, the 2–6 week onset window means the causative drug has often been discontinued (or restarted) before the mechanism is understood.

The structural reason for these failures is precise: IgE testing measures the presence of allergen-specific immunoglobulin E, which is produced when mast cells and basophils sensitise to an antigen via the FcεRI receptor pathway. This pathway requires mast cell IgE crosslinking and produces symptoms within minutes. For reactions that do not use this pathway — that instead use T cell-mediated inflammation, eosinophilic accumulation driven by IL-13/eotaxin-3, immune complex deposition, or neutrophil-mediated gut injury — IgE is typically absent from the primary mechanism. For these conditions, negative IgE testing is not a sensitivity problem — IgE is not the relevant molecule.

> **THE DIAGNOSTIC PARADOX:**
> The standard test for 'allergy' measures IgE-mediated mast cell sensitisation.
> EoE, FPIES, Type III/IV hypersensitivity, and most delayed food reactions do not use mast cell IgE crosslinking.
> A negative IgE test in these patients does not mean no immune reaction.
> It means the reaction is not IgE-mediated — which is exactly what the mechanism predicts.
> **The test is not wrong. It is being applied to the wrong disease.**

The paper is structured as follows. §2 maps the Coombs and Gell hypersensitivity taxonomy to diagnostic tool performance. §3 introduces the innate alarm architecture (ILC2s, TSLP/IL-33/IL-25) common to all type 2 barrier-failure conditions. §4 explains why delayed reactions are delayed. §5 proposes a mechanism-matched diagnostic framework. §§7–10 apply the framework to the four major conditions: EoE (§7), FPIES (§8), delayed drug reactions (§9), and hypersensitivity pneumonitis (§10). §6 addresses neurochemical and hormonal amplification layers — substance P, VIP, and estradiol — which are supported as individual mechanisms but whose integrated role in non-IgE food reactions remains a translational hypothesis rather than established clinical fact. §11 draws cross-system connections, also at the level of mechanistic inference. §12 derives testable predictions. §§13–14 describe open gaps and limitations.

## §2 — Hypersensitivity Types: Mechanisms, Timescales, and Diagnostic Accuracy

The Coombs and Gell classification of hypersensitivity reactions, developed in 1963 and updated since, provides a mechanistic framework that directly predicts diagnostic tool performance. The central clinical problem is that Type I IgE-mediated reactions — the only category for which standard allergy tests are designed — account for a minority of clinically significant immune reactions to food, drugs, and environmental antigens.

| Type | Mechanism | Onset | Examples | IgE testing | Better test |
|------|-----------|-------|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Type I — IgE-mediated** | Allergen → IgE crosslinking on mast cells/basophils → histamine, tryptase, prostaglandins | Minutes (2–30 min) | Anaphylaxis, urticaria, immediate food allergy, allergic rhinitis | Excellent — designed for this | SPT, sIgE, BAT (second-line) |
| **Type II — Cytotoxic (IgG/IgM)** | Ab binds cell surface → complement + NK killing | Hours | Haemolytic transfusion reactions, drug-induced haemolytic anaemia | N/A | Direct Coombs test, drug-specific antibody panels |
| **Type III — Immune complex** | Antigen-antibody complexes deposit in tissues → complement → neutrophil recruitment → inflammation | Hours to days (6–21 days for serum sickness) | Serum sickness, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, some vasculitis | Negative or misleading | Serum precipitins (IgG), complement levels, immune complex assays |
| **Type IV — Cell-mediated (delayed)** | T cell sensitisation → re-exposure → Th1 (IFN-γ) or Th2 (IL-13/IL-5) effector response → tissue inflammation. SP from sensory nerves amplifies via NK1 receptors (neurogenic inflammation). | 48–72 h (Th1); days to >1 week (Th2/eosinophilic) | Contact dermatitis, DRESS, SJS, tuberculin reaction, EoE, allergic contact stomatitis | Negative | Patch testing (contact allergens); HLA pharmacogenomics (drug reactions); elimination + endoscopy (EoE) |
| **Non-IgE food hypersensitivity (mixed/unclear)** | Th2 polarisation without detectable antigen-specific IgE; epithelial barrier failure → ILC2 activation → IL-13/eotaxin-3 → eosinophil/mast cell accumulation | Hours to days; symptoms lag trigger by 24–96 h+ | EoE, FPIES, allergic proctocolitis | Almost always negative | Eotaxin-3, mucosal impedance (EoE); clinical pattern recognition (FPIES); elimination diet + endoscopy |

*Table 1. Types III, IV, and non-IgE food reactions — which include EoE, FPIES, and most delayed reactions — all have poor IgE test accuracy, not because the diseases are poorly characterised, but because the available tests measure a molecule that is absent from the primary pathological mechanism.*

## §3 — The Common Innate Alarm Architecture: ILC2s, Barriers, and Type 2 Convergence

### §3.1 The Epithelial Barrier as the Initiating Structure

Across EoE, atopic dermatitis, asthma, and non-IgE food hypersensitivity, the initiating event is not antigen presentation to T cells. It is disruption of the epithelial barrier — the physical and chemical seal between the external environment and the subepithelial immune compartment. In healthy epithelium, tight junction proteins (claudins, occludin, ZO-1) and structural proteins (filaggrin, desmoglein-1) maintain a barrier that limits antigen penetration, keeping subepithelial dendritic cells in a tolerogenic activation state. When the barrier is disrupted — by genetic variants (multiple EoE susceptibility loci including FLG loss-of-function and the 16p13 risk locus [Kottyan17]; CAPN14 at chromosome 2p21 identified separately), environmental insult (detergents, proteases, acid/pepsin exposure), or infectious damage — the immune default shifts from tolerance to reactivity [Blanchard06].

The initiating signal from disrupted epithelial cells is not antigen itself. It is a set of damage-associated alarm cytokines: TSLP (thymic stromal lymphopoietin), IL-33, and IL-25. These three epithelial-derived cytokines are the upstream instructors of the type 2 immune response. They do not require prior T cell sensitisation. They directly activate the innate immune system — particularly group 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s), mast cells, and dendritic cells — before any adaptive immune response has developed [Obata-Ninomiya24].

### §3.2 ILC2s: The Innate Bridge to Th2 Inflammation

Group 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s) are tissue-resident lymphoid cells found in skin, airways, gut, and esophagus. Unlike Th2 cells, they do not require antigen-specific activation. TSLP, IL-33, and IL-25 — released by damaged epithelium — directly activate ILC2s, which then produce IL-5 and IL-13 in large quantities, with more modest IL-4 output. This ILC2-derived cytokine output initiates the same downstream cascade as a classical Th2 T cell response: IL-5 drives eosinophil maturation and survival; IL-13 drives eotaxin-3 (CCL26) production and subepithelial remodelling; IL-4 promotes IgE class switching in B cells and drives Th2 T cell differentiation [Obata-Ninomiya24].

The clinical significance of ILC2s is that they explain how atopic disease can begin before classical IgE sensitisation is established, and why IgE testing can be negative in active disease. In EoE specifically, ILC2s activated by esophageal TSLP/IL-33 produce IL-13 directly — driving eotaxin-3 secretion and a self-sustaining barrier suppression loop (IL-13 directly suppresses filaggrin and claudin, preventing the barrier from repairing itself) — without requiring IgE-mediated mast cell activation as an intermediate step. ILC2s may participate in disease initiation in barrier-compromised individuals before classical IgE sensitisation is established, though in most established EoE the Th2 adaptive arm is the dominant sustaining mechanism [Nat-CellMolImmunol25].

### §3.3 The Unifying Cascade Across Atopic Conditions

The same innate alarm cascade — barrier disruption → TSLP/IL-33/IL-25 → ILC2 activation → IL-5/IL-13 production → eosinophil recruitment + barrier further suppressed — operates across EoE (esophagus), atopic dermatitis (skin), asthma (airways), and allergic rhinitis (nasal mucosa). The target organ and trigger antigen differ; the initiation architecture is shared. This explains the atopic march — the well-documented tendency for atopic conditions to co-occur and develop sequentially — and provides the mechanistic basis for dupilumab's efficacy as an umbrella therapy across multiple co-existing atopic conditions (blocking the shared IL-4/IL-13 signal) [Manti24].

For diagnostic purposes, the key implication is: a patient with barrier-driven, ILC2-initiated type 2 disease will be IgE-negative to the causative antigen because IgE is downstream of adaptive immune T cell priming, not upstream of innate ILC2 activation. The reaction is genuine, immune-mediated, and driven by IL-13 — but does not require IgE as an intermediate. Testing for it is testing the wrong link in the chain.

## §4 — Why Delayed Reactions Are Delayed: Accumulation Kinetics

### §4.1 The Accumulation Model

The delay between antigen exposure and symptom onset in non-IgE reactions is not poorly understood biology. It is the direct consequence of the cellular recruitment structure of the cascade. In IgE-mediated Type I reactions, the initiating event is mast cell crosslinking, which produces symptoms within minutes because degranulation is a preformed granule release event — no new protein synthesis or cellular recruitment is required. In non-IgE reactions, symptoms require accumulation of inflammatory cells (eosinophils, T cells, neutrophils) or immune complexes to concentrations above a tissue-specific threshold. This accumulation takes time.

| Delay phase | Duration | Process | Clinical implication |
|-------------|----------|---------|---------------------|
| Antigen penetration | Minutes–hours | Food antigen crosses compromised barrier; sub-epithelial DC sampling; ILC2/TSLP activation begins | Immediate oral symptoms (<30 min) imply IgE component. Non-IgE reactions cannot produce symptoms at this timescale. |
| Alarmin cascade | Hours | TSLP/IL-33 → ILC2/mast cell activation → eotaxin-3 secretion begins; IL-5 drives bone marrow eosinophil mobilisation | Serum eotaxin-3 (IL-13-driven, correlates with tissue eosinophilia [Blanchard06]) may rise before the 15 eos/hpf threshold is crossed — proposed biomarker window, not yet validated in human kinetics studies. |
| Eosinophil recruitment | Hours–48 h | Eotaxin-3 gradient → circulating eosinophils migrate into tissue; IL-5 sustains survival; local mast cell SP-amplified degranulation lowers symptom threshold | Tissue eosinophil count rising but may not yet cross 15 eos/hpf diagnostic threshold. Patient symptomatic before diagnosis possible by biopsy. |
| Symptom threshold crossing | 24–96 h post-trigger | Eosinophil density crosses tissue-specific threshold; eosinophil peroxidase and major basic protein damage epithelium → dysphagia, chest pain, food impaction | Patient associates symptoms with recent meal, not meal 1–4 days prior. Food diary misses trigger systematically. |
| Symptom persistence | Days–weeks post-trigger removal | Eosinophil tissue half-life ≈8–12 days; IL-5 signalling decays slowly; barrier repair begins if antigen removed | Symptoms persist after trigger removal. Patient may conclude elimination 'didn't work' and reintroduce. |

*Table 2. Delay kinetics for eosinophilic (non-IgE) reactions. For EoE, the cascade from antigen penetration to threshold crossing typically takes 24–96 hours.*

### §4.2 Threshold Variability and the 'Inconsistent Reaction' Problem

A persistent clinical puzzle in non-IgE reactions is that patients react to a food sometimes but not always. This is not patient inaccuracy. It is the consequence of a threshold that is not fixed but is instead a function of multiple simultaneous inputs: current barrier integrity; baseline eosinophil burden; concurrent inflammatory load; estradiol-mediated mast cell priming state (see §6.2); and SP-driven neurogenic amplification (see §6.1).

This threshold variability is consistent with clinical observations that food challenge threshold doses are lower in patients with active concurrent inflammatory conditions, and with the well-documented cyclical pattern of allergic disease severity in women of reproductive age.

### §4.3 The Food Diary Problem

Food diaries fail systematically for non-IgE reactions — not because patients are inaccurate, but because the diary captures the wrong time window. A diary protocol informed by accumulation kinetics would review food intake from 48–96 hours prior to symptom onset, not the last meal. Dairy and wheat are statistically the most common EoE triggers (responsible for approximately 60% of cases) and are the highest-probability candidates in the shifted window [Kliewer23, Furuta07].

### §5.1 Diagnostic Tools: What Each Test Actually Measures

### §5.2 Component-Resolved Diagnostics: Precision Within IgE Testing

For IgE-mediated reactions, component-resolved diagnostics (CRD) represent a major upgrade over extract-based testing. CRD resolves a critical problem: sensitisation to Cor a 9 (hazelnut storage protein) predicts genuine hazelnut allergy with risk of systemic reaction; sensitisation only to Cor a 1 (Bet v 1 homologue) predicts pollen-related oral allergy syndrome, typically mild [EAACI-MAUG23].

For EoE specifically, CRD remains an adjunctive rather than definitive tool. No prospective RCT of CRD-guided elimination against empiric step-up has been completed. Empiric 1FED (milk elimination) remains the evidence-based first step.

### §5.3 Basophil Activation Testing: A Functional Advance for IgE Reactions

The basophil activation test (BAT) is a flow cytometry assay that measures whether bound IgE is functionally active — whether it actually triggers basophil degranulation at the allergen concentrations tested. As of 2024, BAT has been included in clinical guidelines for food allergy diagnosis. A positive BAT confirms allergy with high certainty (high specificity); a negative BAT does not exclude allergy and may still require OFC [Santos21].

**The limitation for this paper's primary focus:** BAT measures IgE-dependent basophil activation. EoE patients with negative sIgE will also have negative BAT to their trigger foods, because the EoE mechanism does not use IgE-crosslinking of basophils.

## §6 — Neurochemical and Hormonal Amplification Layers

> **Note on evidentiary status:** The mechanisms described in this section — substance P neurogenic inflammation, VIP depletion, and estradiol mast cell priming — are individually supported by replicated human evidence at the molecular level. Their specific contribution to non-IgE food reactions (EoE, FPIES) has not been directly quantified in prospective clinical studies. The claims in §§6.1–6.3 are mechanistic proposals supported by translational evidence, not established determinants of clinical outcome.

### §6.1 Substance P and Neurogenic Inflammation

Substance P (SP) is an 11-amino-acid neuropeptide released from primary sensory nerve terminals at sites of tissue inflammation. SP directly triggers mast cell degranulation independently of IgE via NK1 receptors — neurogenic inflammation: the nervous system amplifying the immune response at the tissue level, independent of the adaptive immune cascade [Payan89].

The SP neurogenic amplification loop explains several clinical observations: why emotional stress lowers allergic thresholds (stress activates sensory nerve SP release via sympathetic NE → β2-AR on sensory neurons); why concurrent illness worsens allergic reactions (tissue SP from infection primes mast cells); and why exercise-induced anaphylaxis occurs with food antigens that are individually tolerated — consistent with the SP neurogenic amplification mechanism, though direct SP measurement in FDEIA has not been performed [Payan89, Said91].

### §6.2 Estradiol and the Female Atopy Bias

Atopic disease shows a well-documented sex ratio reversal across the lifespan: boys have higher rates before puberty; women have higher rates after puberty (approximately 1.5–2× adult prevalence for asthma). This reversal is mechanistically explained by estradiol's direct effect on mast cell biology. Estradiol (E2) at ERα upregulates FcεRI (the IgE high-affinity receptor) expression, increasing IgE-binding site density on the mast cell surface [Forsythe99, Zaitsu08].

Note that EoE itself shows a mild male predominance (approximately 3:1 in some series), likely reflecting the dominance of CAPN14 genetic susceptibility and Th2/eosinophilic (rather than mast cell/IgE-dependent) biology in EoE pathogenesis. The E2 mast cell priming mechanism is most relevant to the IgE-dependent atopic conditions (asthma, rhinitis, eczema) rather than to EoE directly.

### §6.3 VIP and the Loss of Bronchoprotective Parasympathetic Signalling

Vasoactive intestinal peptide (VIP) from parasympathetic nerve terminals in the airway is the primary endogenous bronchoprotective neuropeptide. VIP at VPAC1 and VPAC2 receptors provides: (1) bronchodilation via adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → smooth muscle relaxation; (2) mast cell suppression — VPAC1/2 on airway mast cells raises the degranulation threshold [Said91]; (3) anti-inflammatory signalling — VIP promotes Treg differentiation and suppresses Th1/Th17 polarisation at mucosal surfaces [Gonzalez-Rey06].

In asthma and severe atopic disease, VIPergic nerve density in the airway is reduced — measurable in bronchial biopsies from asthmatic patients and correlating with asthma severity [Ollerenshaw89].

## §7 — Eosinophilic Esophagitis: IgE Testing Predictably Fails

> **A note on taxonomy:** EoE appears in Table 1 within the Type IV (cell-mediated, delayed) row because it shares key features — T cell involvement, delayed kinetics, eosinophilic tissue infiltration, negative IgE testing. However, EoE is not reducible to classical Type IV hypersensitivity. Its primary initiating architecture is epithelial barrier failure triggering innate ILC2 alarm (TSLP/IL-33/IL-25) before antigen-specific T cell priming occurs. This places EoE at the intersection of the Coombs and Gell taxonomy and the newer epithelial barrier disease paradigm. Both frameworks are correct in part; neither fully captures EoE alone.

EoE is the paradigmatic non-IgE food hypersensitivity. IgE testing is predictably, mechanistically negative, because EoE is driven by a barrier-failure → ILC2/Th2 cascade that does not require mast cell IgE crosslinking as an intermediate step. The mechanism is: barrier failure → TSLP/IL-33 alarmin release → ILC2 + Th2 activation → IL-13 → eotaxin-3 (CCL26) → eosinophil recruitment. IgE is not required in this primary pathway [Blanchard06, Furuta07].

The atopy confound: 70–80% of EoE patients are atopic and will test IgE-positive to some foods. In most cases these IgE responses reflect co-existing IgE-mediated atopic sensitisation rather than the EoE pathomechanism itself, though in a subset of patients with atypical EoE the relationship between food-specific IgE and EoE trigger remains under investigation.

The correct diagnostic sequence for EoE: (1) endoscopy + biopsy to confirm diagnosis (≥15 eos/hpf at two or more esophageal levels); (2) step-up dietary elimination beginning with milk (1FED) for 6 weeks; (3) confirm response by endoscopy/biopsy; (4) if no response, advance to 4FED or pharmacotherapy. The 2023 Kliewer et al. Lancet trial (n=129 adults) confirmed that 1FED was non-inferior to 6FED (34% vs 40% histologic remission; the pre-specified non-inferiority margin was met) [Kliewer23, Rothenberg24, Update23]. Dupilumab (FDA 2022) and cendakimab (Phase 3 published 2025) are the approved and emerging biologic options [Dellon22_NEJM, Dellon25_NEJMEvid].

**Upstream diagnostic opportunity:** eotaxin-3 (CCL26) is produced by esophageal epithelial cells in direct response to IL-13 and correlates with tissue eosinophil density [Blanchard06]. A blood draw at day 7 and day 14 of food reintroduction may detect a rising eotaxin-3 signal before symptoms recur and before eosinophil counts cross the 15 eos/hpf diagnostic threshold — a proposed biomarker window not yet validated in a prospective human kinetics study.

## §8 — FPIES: The Non-IgE Enterocolitis

Food protein-induced enterocolitis syndrome (FPIES) is a non-IgE cell-mediated food allergy presenting as profuse, repetitive vomiting 1–4 hours after ingesting a trigger food [Nowak-Wegrzyn17, Anvari24]. The mechanism involves innate immune activation in the gut, primarily neutrophil-mediated (CD11b upregulation on circulating neutrophils is the leading research biomarker candidate), with possible mast cell contribution in atypical presentations, by food antigen without prior IgE sensitisation.

Key diagnostic features: (1) repetitive, projectile vomiting 1–4 hours after trigger ingestion, without urticaria, wheezing, or cutaneous symptoms; (2) profound lethargy and pallor; (3) negative IgE testing; (4) resolution with trigger elimination; (5) confirmation by supervised OFC per FPIES-specific OFC protocols [Nowak-Wegrzyn17]. In acute FPIES, ondansetron is now recommended as first-line management for acute emesis in uncomplicated episodes; epinephrine remains indicated for presentations with haemodynamic compromise.

The most common triggers are cow's milk and soy (infants), followed by rice, oat, fish, and egg. Adult-onset FPIES typically involves crustacean shellfish. Most milk- and soy-triggered paediatric FPIES resolves by age 3–5 years; fish-triggered FPIES tends to have a longer course. No validated diagnostic biomarker currently exists — the field's primary unmet need.

## §9 — Delayed Drug Reactions: Type IV Mechanisms and HLA Pharmacogenomics

### §9.1 DRESS and SJS

Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms (DRESS) has onset 2–6 weeks after drug initiation. Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)/Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN) has a 1–3 week onset window. Both are driven by HLA-restricted T cell recognition of drug-HLA complexes, producing cytokine-mediated tissue destruction without IgE involvement [Pichler03]. IgE is structurally absent. Standard allergy testing is both negative and irrelevant.

### §9.2 HLA Pharmacogenomics: Targeting the Upstream Detection Architecture

HLA pharmacogenomic screening before prescribing is standard of care for abacavir (FDA label 2008) and carbamazepine (FDA label 2007 for Asian populations) and recommended for allopurinol in populations with high B*58:01 prevalence. HLA-B*57:01 screening for abacavir has near-eliminated abacavir hypersensitivity in populations where it is implemented. This is the mechanistically correct intervention: the detection error is identified before exposure, not after.

## §10 — Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis: Type III/IV Accumulation in the Lung

Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP) is a Type III/IV mixed-mechanism lung disease caused by repeated inhalation of organic antigens — thermophilic actinomycetes in mouldy hay (farmer's lung), avian proteins in bird droppings (bird fancier's lung), fungal spores, and many other sources [Selman12]. Serum precipitins are positive in 80–90% of confirmed acute HP cases, though precipitins can also be positive in 30–50% of asymptomatically exposed individuals — a positive result supports but does not establish the diagnosis; clinical context is required.

## §11 — Cross-System Connections

*The connections described in this section are mechanistic inferences drawn from shared biology. Each individual link is supported by published evidence; the integrated clinical implications are proposed rather than demonstrated.*

- **EoE and GERD/LPR:** Acid and pepsin damage to the esophageal epithelial barrier directly impairs barrier integrity, lowering the threshold for food antigen penetration and ILC2/Th2 activation. PPI-responsive EoE is proposed to result from barrier repair via acid suppression. The two conditions mechanistically amplify each other.

- **Antibiotic resistance / microbiome:** Early childhood antibiotic use is among the strongest risk factors for subsequent atopic disease. The mechanism runs through the SCFA → Treg → Th2 axis: antibiotic-induced SCFA-producing microbiome collapse reduces Treg induction, permitting unopposed Th2 skewing. This is the hygiene hypothesis at molecular resolution [Cahenzli13, Ruokoliainen15].

> **BROADER MECHANISTIC HYPOTHESES** *(translational inference level)*
>
> **Autoimmunity:** EoE, FPIES, and delayed food hypersensitivity share the TSLP/IL-33 alarmin axis and ILC2-driven Th2 polarisation with autoimmune conditions. Estradiol amplifies immune reactivity in both Th2-driven atopic disease and Th1-driven autoimmune disease; the phenotype depends on which circuit receives antigen [Elenkov00].
>
> **Depression / chronic pain:** SP neurogenic inflammation amplifies both allergic inflammation (NK1 on mast cells) and central sensitisation (NK1 on spinal cord neurons). Elevated SP in atopic disease is a proposed contributor to comorbid neuropsychiatric symptoms. NK1 antagonists have theoretical anti-inflammatory potential across both circuits.
>
> **Tinnitus / autonomic:** VIP depletion in atopic airway disease is the same neuropeptide deficit implicated in LES relaxation dysregulation and potentially in Eustachian tube dysfunction. Whether treating the neuroinflammatory component improves tinnitus in atopic patients is untested.
>
> *Each individual connection above is supported by published mechanistic evidence; the integrated clinical implications are proposed and have not been prospectively validated.*

## §13 — Open Gaps

- **FPIES diagnostic biomarker:** No validated blood-based biomarker exists for FPIES diagnosis or monitoring. TARC/CCL17 and TNF-α surges during OFC are candidate biomarkers (small case series data; reported in a November 2024 paper describing findings from the 2022 NIAID FPIES workshop) but require prospective validation. P4 above targets this gap.
- **BAT standardisation:** BAT lacks standardised protocols across laboratories and clinically validated cutoffs for most allergens beyond peanut. The 2024 inclusion in clinical guidelines is a milestone; the standardisation work required for broad clinical adoption is ongoing.
- **ILC2-specific therapeutic targeting:** No approved therapy currently specifically targets ILC2s. Tezepelumab (anti-TSLP, approved for asthma) and anti-IL-33 biologics (itepekimab) block upstream ILC2 activators, but ILC2-intrinsic markers for direct targeting have not been identified.
- **NK1 antagonists in allergy:** Aprepitant is FDA-approved as an antiemetic (SP in the emesis reflex). Its anti-inflammatory potential via NK1 on mast cells, eosinophils, and sensory neurons has not been tested in clinical allergy or EoE trials. P3 above targets this gap.
- **Eotaxin-3 clinical cutoff for EoE:** No validated cutoff exists. A prospective study correlating serial eotaxin-3 with simultaneous EREFS and biopsy would establish the first non-invasive disease activity biomarker for EoE.
- **Hormonal modulation of EoE symptom severity:** The proposed mechanism is E2 effects on esophageal sensory nerve threshold or immune cell trafficking (not mast cell FcεRI priming, which is not the primary EoE pathway). Whether menstrual cycle phase predicts EoE symptom severity has not been prospectively characterised. P5 above targets this gap.
- **CRD utility in EoE:** CRD-guided dietary management in EoE has not been validated in a prospective RCT against empiric step-up elimination. P6 above targets this gap.

## §14 — Limitations

This paper is a mechanistic synthesis and narrative review, not a systematic review. The literature search was targeted rather than exhaustive. The reference list (n=27) is selective.

Several mechanisms are well-supported at the individual molecular step level (SP/NK1 on mast cells: replicated human evidence; E2/FcεRI upregulation via ERα: replicated human evidence [Zaitsu08]) but the full integrated cascade — particularly the combined SP×E2 threshold interaction, and the VIP depletion → mast cell priming cascade in asthma — is mechanistically supported but not yet fully validated clinically in EoE and FPIES specifically.

BAT data cited here pertains primarily to IgE-mediated reactions. The argument that BAT is irrelevant for non-IgE conditions is mechanistically correct but the evidence base for BAT performance specifically in EoE patients (as expected negatives) is limited; no large prospective study of BAT in EoE patients against confirmed trigger identification has been published.

CRD for EoE is an area of active investigation and the conclusion that it is 'not yet ready to replace empiric elimination' reflects the state of the literature as of early 2026 and may evolve.
