## Abstract

Type 2 diabetes (T2D) is mechanistically distinct from Type 1. Where T1D is autoimmune destruction of beta-cell mass, T2D is a progressive desensitisation of the insulin signalling gate combined with beta-cell compensatory failure under chronic metabolic overload. The disease has a dual threshold structure: peripheral insulin resistance followed by beta-cell exhaustion, with clinical diabetes emerging when both are sufficiently impaired [DeFronzo09]. The primary driver of insulin resistance is not body weight per se but ectopic lipid accumulation — intramyocellular lipid, hepatic steatosis (MASLD), and pancreatic fat — which activates the IRS-1 serine phosphorylation cascade decoupling the insulin receptor from glucose transport [SamuelShulman16]. This ectopic lipid hypothesis unifies the metabolic syndrome constellation as expressions of a single upstream overflow state.

The central nervous system is the primary integrator of energy balance signals. The hypothalamic appetite gate (NPY/AgRP versus POMC/CART) integrates hormonal, metabolic, and circadian inputs to set and adjust energy balance [Schwartz00]; mesolimbic dopamine D2 downregulation from chronic hedonic eating raises the reward threshold [Kenny11]. GLP-1 receptor agonists work through both the pancreatic gate and central circuits simultaneously [Drucker06]. Tirzepatide's dual GLP-1/GIP agonism adds a non-redundant central pathway through GIPR-expressing median eminence oligodendrocytes, potentiating the GLP-1 anorectic effect synergistically *(Proposed: rodent models with supporting human GIPR expression data)* [Zhang21_GIPR, Jastreboff22, SURMOUNT5_2025].

Sleep, circadian disruption, and stress constitute a further mechanistic tier — beyond the primary ectopic lipid cascade, central appetite dysregulation, and hormonal modulation — that activates the same insulin signalling failure cascade through three distinct entry points: the pancreatic beta-cell's autonomous circadian clock (CLOCK/BMAL1) drives rhythmic transcription of the insulin secretory machinery and its disruption specifically impairs first-phase insulin secretion in rodent models [Marcheva10, Perelis15]; sleep deprivation dysregulates ghrelin and leptin and activates the HPA axis [Spiegel04, StOnge23]; and obstructive sleep apnea generates nightly FFA and cortisol surges via intermittent hypoxia [Chopra17].

T2D is an ectopic fat problem that produces a glucose symptom — a framing that applies most directly to the majority of T2D cases in individuals with overweight or obesity; lean T2D, affecting approximately 20–25% of cases, involves overlapping mechanisms including primary beta-cell reserve insufficiency and requires separate characterisation. Six testable predictions are derived.

Claims in this paper span all three evidence tiers. The ectopic lipid mechanism and clinical trial outcomes are **Established**; central appetite circuitry, hormonal modulation, and metabolic memory are **Supported**; beta-cell clock disruption in humans, the GIPR oligodendrocyte pathway, and cross-system connections are **Proposed**. Inline tier labels throughout the text identify the evidentiary status of each major claim.

**Keywords:** type 2 diabetes; insulin resistance; ectopic lipid; MASLD; GLP-1; GIP; tirzepatide; hypothalamic appetite; BMAL1; circadian clock; obstructive sleep apnea; estradiol; PCOS; metabolic memory; beta-cell

## Methods and Scope

This paper presents a mechanistic framework for T2D and metabolic syndrome. Literature was identified through targeted searches of PubMed and Google Scholar (January 2000 to March 2026), supplemented by backward citation of landmark papers and primary sources for specific mechanistic claims. Search terms included combinations of: type 2 diabetes, insulin resistance, ectopic lipid, MASLD, GLP-1, GIP, tirzepatide, hypothalamic appetite, beta-cell clock, circadian disruption, obstructive sleep apnea, estradiol, PCOS, and metabolic memory. Studies were prioritised by design: RCTs and prospective cohort studies for drug mechanism and clinical outcome claims; replicated human translational studies for mechanistic claims; animal models only where human evidence was unavailable, with explicit labelling. Where evidence conflicted, the higher-quality design and more recent consensus were cited with the conflict noted. No new experimental data are reported.

**Inclusion criteria:** studies were included if they (1) directly established a named mechanism, quantified a clinical outcome, or characterised a biological pathway relevant to the paper's framework; (2) were published in peer-reviewed journals or as pre-registered trial reports; and (3) were the highest-quality available source for a specific claim. Review articles were used only where primary mechanistic papers were unavailable or where a review was the canonical synthesis of a large literature. Animal studies were included only where human evidence was unavailable, and are labelled explicitly. **Exclusion criteria:** case reports, editorials without data, and studies whose primary findings were superseded by higher-quality evidence. Tier assignments followed operational rules: *Established* required at least one RCT or well-powered prospective cohort study with a direct human outcome; *Supported* required replicated human translational or mechanistic studies without a definitive outcome trial; *Proposed* required at least one peer-reviewed mechanistic study plus biological plausibility, with no direct human causal validation. Where a claim spanned multiple tiers, the more conservative tier was assigned. No statistical pooling was performed.

Evidence tiers: **Established** = replicated human trials or well-powered cohort studies · **Supported** = consistent human translational evidence, no definitive outcome trial · **Proposed** = rodent-derived, integrative inferences, or unvalidated hypotheses. Cross-system connections (§10) are Proposed throughout.

## §1 — T2D vs. T1D: Two Structurally Different Diseases

Type 1 diabetes is autoimmune destruction of beta-cells with no insulin resistance. Type 2 diabetes is not a destruction problem but a desensitisation problem: beta-cells are present and initially functional, and failure begins in peripheral tissues where the insulin signalling gate becomes progressively unresponsive. Beta-cells compensate by hypersecretion — sometimes for decades — until they begin failing under glucotoxicity, lipotoxicity, and IAPP (islet amyloid polypeptide)-mediated amyloid accumulation.

T2D is a sequential dual gate failure: (1) peripheral insulin receptor signalling efficiency and (2) beta-cell functional reserve. Clinical diabetes emerges when both are sufficiently impaired — in common T2D both components are typically required, which explains why identical degrees of obesity do not invariably produce T2D, and why individuals with adequate beta-cell genetic reserve can maintain euglycaemia despite significant insulin resistance [DeFronzo09]. The dominant genetic risk loci include TCF7L2 and KCNJ11 — predominantly beta-cell function genes rather than peripheral insulin signalling genes.

> The diagnostic threshold of fasting glucose ≥126 mg/dL represents approximately 50% beta-cell mass loss and a decade or more of subclinical insulin resistance. Addressing the underlying insulin resistance alongside glucose-lowering therapy is mechanistically important; pharmacotherapy that targets only the glucose symptom without reducing the lipotoxic load leaves the upstream structural failure unmodified.

## §2 — The Ectopic Lipid Hypothesis: Root Cause

### 2.1 IRS-1 Serine Phosphorylation: The Gate Mechanism

The core mechanistic proposal of this paper — the ectopic lipid hypothesis — holds that when adipose storage capacity is exceeded, FFAs spill into circulation and accumulate in liver, muscle, and beta-cells. In skeletal muscle — responsible for approximately 80% of post-prandial glucose disposal — excess FFA generates diacylglycerol (DAG), which activates PKC-theta (muscle-specific) to serine-phosphorylate IRS-1, and ceramide, which activates both PKC-zeta (phosphorylating IRS-1 on Ser307) and protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A, dephosphorylating Akt at Thr308) through parallel routes [SamuelShulman16]. Both lipid intermediates converge on blockade of PI3K/Akt signalling — GLUT4 vesicles remain intracellular and glucose uptake fails despite elevated plasma insulin [DeFronzo09].

> The insulin signal is sent normally. The gate has been decoupled from its downstream machinery by lipid intermediates. Elevated plasma insulin in early T2D reflects compensatory hypersecretion by beta-cells responding to a desensitised peripheral receptor — not a primary secretory defect at this stage; genuine secretory failure emerges later as lipotoxicity and glucotoxicity accumulate (§2.3, §6).

### 2.2 Hepatic Steatosis and Selective Insulin Resistance

Excess FFA drives MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease — replacing NAFLD per Rinella23 consensus, adopted in 2024 EASL-EASD-EASO guidelines). Hepatic insulin resistance is paradoxically selective: gluconeogenesis suppression is lost (fasting hyperglycaemia) while lipogenesis via mTORC1/SREBP-1c is preserved and may be hyperactivated [Yecies11, Brown08]. In hepatocytes the relevant PKC isoform is PKC-epsilon (not PKC-theta). This explains why hepatic insulin resistance simultaneously produces fasting hyperglycaemia and hypertriglyceridaemia.

Resmetirom (Rezdiffra), approved March 2024 for noncirrhotic MASH (the inflammatory progressive subtype of MASLD, formerly NASH) with F2–F3 fibrosis — the first pharmacotherapy for this condition [Harrison24]. Potential T2D insulin resistance benefit in patients with concurrent MASLD is supported by MAESTRO-NASH secondary endpoints but not established as a standalone T2D indication.

### 2.3 Pancreatic Fat and Beta-Cell Lipotoxicity

Excess FFA drives ceramide accumulation → ER stress → apoptosis. IAPP oligomers formed under chronic hypersecretion and lipotoxic stress are directly cytotoxic through membrane disruption and ER stress induction — the mechanism that makes late T2D partially irreversible [Westermark11].

### 2.4 Visceral vs. Subcutaneous Adipose

Visceral adipose has higher lipolytic activity, greater inflammatory cytokine secretion (IL-6, TNF-alpha, resistin), lower adiponectin, and portal drainage delivering FFAs directly to the liver [Frayn02]. Waist circumference is a stronger T2D predictor than BMI [Carey97]. **Critical experimental evidence:** surgical removal of ~10 kg of subcutaneous adipose by liposuction produced no improvement in insulin sensitivity, plasma glucose, lipid profiles, blood pressure, or inflammatory markers [Klein04] — because the mechanistically relevant variable is visceral adipose mass and its FFA overflow rate, not total fat mass.

## §3 — The Central Nervous System Layer

### 3.1 The Hypothalamic Appetite Gate: NPY/AgRP vs. POMC/CART

The CNS is the primary integrator of energy balance signals, with peripheral hormonal systems (§4) and circadian inputs (§5) providing significant modulation. The arcuate nucleus contains two opposing populations [Schwartz00]:

- **NPY/AgRP neurons (orexigenic):** activated by ghrelin, low leptin, low insulin, low glucose. AgRP antagonises melanocortin receptors MC3R/MC4R.
- **POMC/CART neurons (anorexigenic):** POMC → alpha-MSH → MC4R activation. Activated by leptin, insulin, GLP-1, high glucose.

Hypothalamic insulin resistance — involving serine phosphorylation of neuronal IRS-2 through mTORC1/S6K1 feedback under chronic nutrient excess (distinct in isoform and trigger from peripheral IRS-1) — impairs POMC/CART activation. Leptin resistance arises through TLR4-mediated activation and ER stress in POMC neurons from chronic saturated FFA exposure [Milanski09, Thaler12]. These represent dysregulated feedback mechanisms operating at the neuroendocrine level, not volitional failures.

### 3.2 GLP-1 Central Mechanism and Tirzepatide *(Proposed for GIPR circuit)*

GLP-1R expressed on POMC neurons in arcuate nucleus and NTS/area postrema [Drucker06]. GLP-1 agonists suppress NPY/AgRP and amplify the satiety signal.

*(Proposed: mechanism established in rodent models with supporting human GIPR expression data; human circuit-level validation pending.)* Tirzepatide adds GIPR co-agonism. GIPR signalling in median eminence oligodendrocytes is proposed to potentiate GLP-1R anorectic effect at arcuate nucleus axons [Zhang21_GIPR]. Whether this explains tirzepatide's clinical superiority in humans remains under investigation.

**Key trial data:**
- SURMOUNT-1 (n=2,539, 72 weeks): tirzepatide 15mg → 20.9% mean weight loss, 91% reached ≥5% [Jastreboff22]
- SURMOUNT-5 head-to-head: tirzepatide significantly greater weight reduction vs semaglutide 2.4mg [SURMOUNT5_2025]
- 3-year prediabetes extension: 94% relative T2D risk reduction (HR 0.06)

Whether tirzepatide achieves greater insulin sensitivity improvement per unit weight lost than semaglutide — consistent with peripheral GIP-mediated sensitising — remains an open question (→ P2, §11).

### 3.3 Mesolimbic Dopamine: Hedonic Eating

Chronic palatable food exposure reduces D2 receptor expression in nucleus accumbens — the same tolerance mechanism as substance use disorders [Johnson10, Kenny11]. GLP-1 agonists partially correct the mesolimbic setpoint via central GLP-1R on dopaminergic and limbic circuits.

## §4 — The Hormonal Layer: E2, Testosterone, PCOS, and Cortisol

### 4.1 Estradiol and Insulin Sensitivity *(Supported)*

E2 supports insulin sensitivity via ERalpha signalling [Mauvais-Jarvis13]:
- ERalpha → promotes IRS-1, PI3K-p85, and GLUT4 expression in muscle
- E2 supports mitochondrial biogenesis via PGC-1alpha; E2 loss → reduced fat oxidation → intramyocellular lipid accumulation
- E2 promotes subcutaneous fat deposition; E2 loss → visceral shift → FFA flux to liver

> **THE MENOPAUSE DOUBLE HIT:** E2 loss (1) removes insulin receptor signalling support in muscle and (2) shifts fat to visceral distribution — both independently increasing insulin resistance [Mauvais-Jarvis13]. Women's T2D incidence accelerates post-menopausally for hormonal, not purely ageing, reasons.

### 4.2 PCOS: The Bidirectional Loop *(Supported)*

Insulin resistance → hyperinsulinaemia → excess androgen production → further insulin resistance via AR on muscle → more hyperinsulinaemia. Self-reinforcing once established [Dunaif97, Corbould08].

**Treatment insight:** reducing hyperinsulinaemia is the mechanistically correct target. Metformin has the strongest evidence for normalising androgens and restoring menstrual regularity. GLP-1 agonists show increasing evidence but less established than metformin for menstrual restoration [Tao23].

### 4.3 Testosterone and Insulin Sensitivity in Men *(Supported)*

Low testosterone → hypogonadism-associated sarcopenia (compounded by age-related muscle loss) → reduced GLUT4 density → impaired glucose clearance; and separately promotes visceral adipogenesis [Grossmann14]. Bidirectional loop: insulin resistance → hyperinsulinaemia → LH suppression → reduced testosterone → further insulin resistance.

### 4.4 Cortisol and the HPA-Metabolic Axis *(Supported)*

GR activation of PEPCK and glucose-6-phosphatase → hepatic glucose output [Vegiopoulos07]. Cortisol also stimulates visceral adipose lipolysis and GR-mediated visceral fat accumulation — compounding the ectopic lipid cascade.

## §5 — Sleep, Circadian Disruption, and Metabolic Disease

### 5.1 The Pancreatic Beta-Cell Clock *(Supported in rodent models; human islet validation is an open gap — see §12)*

Beta-cells contain an autonomous circadian clock (CLOCK/BMAL1/PDX1) driving rhythmic transcription of the insulin secretory machinery: Vamp2, Syntaxin1, Snap25, calcium signalling components [Marcheva10, Perelis15]. Beta-cell-specific Bmal1 ablation causes severe glucose intolerance from defective **first-phase** secretion (the 0–10 min spike) without affecting total insulin content [Perelis15]. The clock coordinates insulin release; it does not control insulin production. IL-1beta in T2D-related inflammation disrupts beta-cell BMAL1 via SIRT1 impairment — creating a positive feedback loop [Qian21].

### 5.2 Sleep Deprivation: Three Pathways

- **Ghrelin/leptin dysregulation:** brief sleep restriction elevates ghrelin and suppresses leptin, driving overconsumption [Spiegel04]
- **HPA activation:** elevated evening cortisol → hepatic gluconeogenesis and visceral fat accumulation
- **Direct insulin resistance:** 6 weeks of mild sleep restriction (6.2 hrs/night) → 14.8% insulin resistance increase overall; 20.1% in postmenopausal women — without significant body weight change [StOnge23]

### 5.3 Obstructive Sleep Apnea *(Proposed for downstream IRS-1 mechanism)*

OSA present in >85% of obese T2D patients in polysomnographic studies [Foster09], majority undiagnosed. CPAP withdrawal trial: OSA recurrence dynamically increased nocturnal FFAs (p=0.007), glucose (p=0.028), cortisol (p=0.037) [Chopra17]. *(Proposed: upstream FFA/cortisol surge established; downstream IRS-1 step is mechanistic inference not yet directly demonstrated.)*

### 5.4 Shift Work and Circadian Misalignment *(Established for T2D risk; Supported for mechanism)*

NHS I+II cohorts (n>170,000, 18–20 years): dose-response T2D risk with rotating night shift work [Pan11]. Laboratory isolation of misalignment from sleep deprivation: misalignment alone → 5.6% postprandial glucose increase, doubled insulin sensitivity reduction [Morris15]. Replication in female participants important given sex-specific hormonal interactions (§4).

Fasting insulin elevated, glucose normal. Hypothalamic leptin resistance and beta-cell clock disruption begin. |

## §7 — Metabolic Memory: The Legacy of Prior Hyperglycaemia *(Established)*

The legacy effect: tight glycaemic control early → substantially lower complication rates decades later, even if control subsequently deteriorates (DCCT and UKPDS RCT data) [DCCT93, UKPDS98]. Mechanism — epigenetic structural update [ElOsta08]:

- **H3K4me1 at NF-kB promoter:** written by SET7/9 (SETD7) during hyperglycaemia; persists after glucose normalisation due to insufficient demethylation → elevated NF-kB-driven inflammatory gene expression [ElOsta08]
- **Mitochondrial DNA damage:** persistent mitochondrial superoxide overproduction continues activating inflammatory and apoptotic pathways [Brownlee05]
- **MicroRNA profiles:** multiple NF-kB pathway miRNAs altered by prior hyperglycaemia persist after normalisation

> Early tight control prevents the epigenetic update. Late tight control cannot reverse it. This is mechanistically consistent with the UKPDS and DCCT legacy effects. **The optimal window is prediabetes to early T2D** — before epigenetic remodelling is established and before beta-cell mass loss crosses the irreversibility threshold.

## §8 — The Reversibility Question

### 8.1 What Is Reversible

DiRECT trial: 46% remission at 1 year, 36% at 2 years [Lean18]. 5-year extension: 13% sustained remission [Lean24]. T2D is not inherently progressive — it is progression-by-default under an obesogenic environment.

| Component | Reversibility | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Peripheral insulin resistance** | Reversible | Hepatic insulin sensitivity normalises within ~7 days of aggressive caloric restriction [Lim11]; full muscle recovery takes weeks to months |
| **Hepatic steatosis (MASLD)** | Highly reversible | Hepatic fat normalisation is the fastest-responding component — achievable within ~7 days of very low calorie diet [Lim11], preceding muscle recovery by weeks |
| **Beta-cell function** | Partially reversible | If lipotoxic stress removed before significant apoptosis; correlates with pancreatic fat reduction [Taylor13] |
| **Hypothalamic setpoint** | Partially reversible | GLP-1R agonism activates POMC/CART; sustained fat loss reduces central leptin resistance [Schwartz00, Milanski09]; degree of correction is weight-loss-dependent |

### 8.2 What Is Not Reversible

- **Beta-cell mass loss from apoptosis:** secretory reserve cannot be restored once sufficient mass is lost
- **Metabolic memory:** H3K4me1 and other epigenetic marks are durable [ElOsta08]; glucose normalisation prevents further accumulation but does not erase existing marks
- **Advanced AGE crosslinks in vascular collagen:** chemically irreversible

> The 'diabetes is irreversible' clinical framing is incorrect as a universal claim. The reversibility profile is time-dependent. After significant beta-cell loss and metabolic memory establishment, the disease shifts from reversible ectopic lipid overflow to structurally locked failure.

## §10 — Cross-System Connections

*The following connections are mechanistic inferences drawn from shared biology. Each individual link is supported by published evidence; the integrated clinical implications are proposed rather than demonstrated.*

**CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE (Established):** T2D confers ~2–4x elevated CV event risk independent of other risk factors [Haffner98]. AGE-RAGE endothelial activation, hyperglycaemia-driven ROS, and FFA-driven systemic inflammation degrade endothelial function.

**ALZHEIMER'S DISEASE (Proposed):** Hippocampal insulin resistance (IRS-1/2/PKC) is consistent with impaired AMPA receptor trafficking and synaptic consolidation in rodent models [Biessels15]; human evidence is primarily epidemiological. Post-menopausal women with T2D face disproportionately elevated Alzheimer's risk: peripheral insulin resistance increases, hippocampal E2 support is removed, and ectopic lipid accumulation accelerates — all simultaneously.

**STROKE (Supported):** Hyperglycaemia at stroke onset is independently associated with worse functional outcomes and higher mortality [Capes01], accelerates the ischaemic cascade, worsens reperfusion injury through AGE-compromised BBB integrity, and expands the infarct core faster.

**CANCER (Proposed):** Hyperinsulinaemia is a proposed tumour growth driver via IGF-1 receptor cross-activation, supported by cell line evidence and epidemiological associations; causal directionality requires further validation.

**PCOS AND AUTOIMMUNITY (Proposed):** The PCOS loop creates a systemic inflammatory state. Women with PCOS have elevated rates of Hashimoto's thyroiditis — consistent with chronic visceral adipose-derived IL-6 and TNF-alpha degrading immune tolerance margins.

## §12 — Open Gaps

- **Human islet clock gene characterisation:** Direct transcriptomic quantification of BMAL1, CLOCK, CRY1/2, PER1/2/3 in human T2D islets vs matched controls has not been performed at sufficient scale. Whether beta-cell clock disruption is a consistent causally relevant feature of human T2D — as established in rodents — remains unresolved. This is a prerequisite gap before P1 and P3 can be fully grounded in human biology.

- **Beta-cell clock recovery:** Whether metabolic normalisation (via GLP-1 agonist or bariatric surgery) restores BMAL1/PDX1 expression and first-phase secretion in humans has not been directly demonstrated. Cannot be cleanly addressed until the characterisation gap above is resolved.

- **GIPR central mechanism in humans:** CNS-GIPR knockout establishes that central GIPR regulates body weight in rodents [Zhang21_GIPR]; the oligodendrocyte-mediated mechanism proposed to explain tirzepatide's superiority requires human neuroimaging or circuit-level evidence.

- **Resmetirom + GLP-1 combination:** No prospective RCT published. Mechanistic case for complementarity is strong but clinically unvalidated.

- **Lean T2D phenotype:** ~20–25% of T2D in normal-BMI individuals [Logue11]. Mechanisms require separate characterisation from the ectopic lipid framework.

- **Circadian intervention RCT:** No trial has directly tested time-restricted eating, optimised sleep scheduling, or chronotherapy on glycaemic outcomes in T2D with mechanistic endpoints.

- **E2 therapy for T2D prevention:** Formulation-dependent divergence in trial data — combined HRT no benefit; estrogen-only has shown signals of reduced T2D incidence in some analyses; KEEPS showed improved insulin sensitivity. A definitive trial of transdermal estradiol in early perimenopause with prediabetes has not been conducted.

---

## §13 — Limitations

This paper is a mechanistic synthesis and narrative review, not a systematic review. The beta-cell clock data are primarily from mouse models; direct demonstration that circadian misalignment impairs first-phase secretion via BMAL1/PDX1 in living humans has not been performed. The GIPR oligodendrocyte mechanism has been established in rodent CNS-GIPR knockout models — whether this is the primary driver of tirzepatide's clinical superiority over semaglutide remains under investigation [Zhang21_GIPR].

Cross-system connections (§10) are tier-labelled throughout; none should be read as demonstrated causal relationships. The mechanistic framework applies most directly to T2D in individuals with overweight or obesity; lean T2D (~20–25% of cases) involves overlapping mechanisms not fully captured by the ectopic lipid hypothesis.

---

## §14 — Conclusion

Type 2 diabetes, in its common obesity-associated form, is best understood as a dual-threshold ectopic lipid disease: when adipose storage capacity is exceeded, FFA overflow into liver, muscle, and beta-cells initiates a cascade of IRS-1/2 serine phosphorylation, selective hepatic insulin resistance, beta-cell lipotoxicity, and IAPP-mediated cytotoxicity. Clinical diabetes emerges when both peripheral insulin resistance and beta-cell functional reserve are sufficiently impaired.

**Strongest evidence:** the ectopic lipid mechanism [DeFronzo09, SamuelShulman16]; selective hepatic insulin resistance [Yecies11, Brown08]; GLP-1 agonist cardiorenal protection [Marso16_LEADER, Perkovic24]; SGLT2 inhibitor cardiac and renal outcomes [Zinman15, McMurray19]; metabolic memory [ElOsta08, DCCT93, UKPDS98]; T2D reversibility [Lean18, Lean24, Lim11].

**Weakest links:** beta-cell clock disruption in humans (lacks direct transcriptomic validation); GIPR oligodendrocyte mechanism (rodent models only); cross-system connections to AD, cancer, and autoimmunity (epidemiologically supported, not causally demonstrated). All labelled (Proposed) and named in §12.

**Highest-value next experiments (§11):** P1 and P3 are immediately feasible. P2 would resolve the GIPR peripheral sensitisation question. P5 would test whether targeting two distinct ectopic lipid pathways is complementary. These predictions provide a direct empirical route from the framework to falsifiable clinical outcomes — results would either strengthen or substantially revise the multi-layer model presented here.
