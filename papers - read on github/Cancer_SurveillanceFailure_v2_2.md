## Abstract

Cancer is a surveillance failure problem. Every hallmark of cancer [1,2,3] — uncontrolled proliferation, immune evasion, metastasis, metabolic reprogramming — reflects the disabling of a detection checkpoint. This paper applies a unified detection-architecture framework across the complete biology of cancer initiation, progression, and systemic cascade. I characterise tumor suppressor networks as a redundant quiescence barrier whose sequential erosion is structurally consistent with Vogelstein’s empirically established 5–7 hit requirement. Metastasis is formalised here as a second, independent positional threshold collapse (epithelial-mesenchymal transition) distinct from carcinogenesis. Immune checkpoint blockade is mechanistically grounded in a tumor mutational burden threshold above which neoantigen recognition activates immune surveillance. A tumor-brain cascade insufficiently integrated into oncology practice is described through the IDO/kynurenine pathway, with two distinct neurotoxic arms. Exercise is characterised as a four-channel immune surveillance support architecture. A surveillance-derived biomarker panel is proposed as a minimal integrated monitoring framework. Seven testable predictions are derived directly from the architecture.

**Keywords:** cancer immunosurveillance; tumor microenvironment; IDO/kynurenine; tumor mutational burden; HPA axis; exercise oncology; cancer-related fatigue; autonomic nervous system; immune checkpoint blockade; systems oncology

## Methods and Scope

This paper presents a novel detection-architecture framework applied to cancer biology. The central contribution is analytical: a unified structural model that maps carcinogenesis, metastasis, immune evasion, and the tumor-brain-autonomic cascade as a single integrated detection-failure system, and derives from that model a set of testable predictions and a surveillance-based biomarker panel not currently used in clinical practice. Seven explicit predictions are stated, each specifying the expected direction of effect, the patient subgroup in which it should be largest, and what would falsify it. Literature was identified through targeted searches of PubMed and Google Scholar. Search terms included: cancer immunosurveillance, tumor microenvironment, IDO1/kynurenine pathway, tumor mutational burden and ICB response, sympathetic nervous system and cancer, HPA axis and immune function, exercise and NK cells, cancer-related fatigue mechanisms, beta-adrenergic tumor signalling, circadian immune regulation, TREM2 tumor-associated macrophages, perioperative beta-blockade. Evidence supporting the framework is categorised using a three-tier system: Tier A (replicated human evidence, mechanistically confirmed); Tier B (mechanistically supported, consistent but not fully replicated); Tier C (integrative synthesis, each component individually supported, unified cascade not yet demonstrated in humans). No new experimental data are reported.

## §1 — The Institutional Problem Medicine Has Not Solved

Cancer is a detection failure problem. Every hallmark of cancer [1,2,3] — uncontrolled proliferation, immune evasion, metastasis, metabolic reprogramming — is a mechanism by which cells disable a detection checkpoint. The tumor suppressor network is a detection architecture. The immune system is a detection architecture. The adhesion network is a detection architecture. Each one asks the same structural question: does this cell belong here, doing this, at this rate? Cancer is what happens when the answers to those questions stop being enforced.

This paper applies that framing systematically. The body is one distributed system running the same operations across every organ with innervation: signals propagate, gates control passage, surveillance detects deviation, state accumulates, and the network updates based on outcomes. The split between oncology, immunology, neurology, and endocrinology is an institutional boundary. The body does not have that wall.

Three system loops are mechanistically integrated into tumor biology that oncology has been slow to fully incorporate. First, the hormonal and autonomic nervous systems: sympathetic nerve terminals directly innervate solid tumors; parasympathetic/vagal fibers reach most visceral organs; sensory nerves are actively recruited by some tumors (perineural invasion). Second, the brain-immune cascade runs bidirectionally: tumor → cytokines → IDO activation → kynurenine → brain; and brain → HPA → cortisol → immunosuppression → tumor. Third, the exercise axis: physical activity mechanistically supports immune surveillance through at least four parallel channels — NK cell mobilization, cortisol reduction, mTOR-metabolic competition, and the cholinergic anti-inflammatory reflex — all of which inactivity simultaneously suppresses.

## §2 — Carcinogenesis as Surveillance Threshold Crossing

### §2.1 The Proliferative Signaling Network as a Threshold System

The intracellular signaling cascade governing cell proliferation (EGFR → RAS → RAF → MEK → ERK; PI3K → AKT → mTOR) is a threshold system. Each component propagates activation to the next. The tumor suppressor ensemble maintains an energy barrier that keeps this cascade quiescent under normal conditions. The cell proliferates when cumulative suppressor losses degrade that barrier below the activation threshold.

The mTOR node deserves specific attention: mTORC1 is the master anabolic gate in normal cells, activated by amino acids, insulin/IGF-1, and growth factors, and suppressed by AMPK under energy deficit. In cancer, PI3K/AKT/mTOR is one of the most frequently mutated pathways — PTEN loss (which removes the PI3K suppressor), PIK3CA gain-of-function, and AKT amplification all produce constitutive mTOR activation. This means cancer cells are locked in anabolic mode regardless of energy state, continuously consuming glucose, amino acids, and lipid precursors. This is the metabolic basis of the Warburg effect and cancer cachexia — the tumor is running AMPK-suppressed, mTOR-driven biosynthesis at maximum rate while competing for the same metabolic supply as normal tissue [8].

Normal tissue operates with a large safety margin: the combined barrier maintained by the intact suppressor network substantially exceeds the minimum required to keep the proliferative cascade quiescent. Cancer requires hitting the suppressors enough times — in the right order — to erode that margin to zero.

Each tumor suppressor contributes to the energy barrier maintaining quiescence. Losing a suppressor reduces that barrier by a quantifiable amount.

### §2.3 Vogelstein’s Multi-Hit Model: Structural Consistency

Vogelstein and colleagues established empirically that carcinogenesis requires 5–7 sequential somatic mutations for colorectal cancer, with a specific ordered sequence (APC → KRAS → SMAD4 → TP53 + additional hits) [4,5]. The surveillance threshold framework is structurally consistent with this observation: the suppressor architecture has approximately seven meaningful contributors, and the observed requirement of 5–7 hits to achieve malignancy is consistent with needing to erode most of them. This is a heuristic consistency check, not a mechanistic prediction or forward derivation — nearly any multi-component redundant system would show structural consistency with a multi-hit requirement. The framework cannot forward-derive N_hits from first principles without independent measurement of individual barrier contributions from binding energetics, and makes no claim to do so.

Why 5–7 hits? Because the suppressor architecture has that many meaningful contributors. Why ordered? Because each hit must both degrade the barrier and provide a clonal sweep to set up the next hit. Why rare despite lifelong mutation accumulation? Because each individual hit is reversible — the cell dies or arrests. Only when a surviving clone accumulates all required hits does cancer initiate.

### §2.4 Two Failure Modes: Gate Removal and Gain of Function

TUMOR SUPPRESSOR LOSS (gate removal): The tumor suppressor is the surveillance gate. Losing p53 removes the apoptosis gate entirely — DNA-damaged cells are no longer detected and eliminated. The two-hit hypothesis follows from the redundancy structure: one intact allele maintains partial gate function [6].

ONCOGENE ACTIVATION (gain of function): An oncogene mutation (KRAS G12D, EGFR amplification, MYC overexpression) creates a permanently active proliferative signal that bypasses the gating architecture entirely. No amount of intact gating can suppress a constitutively active proliferative driver. Oncogenes require only one mutation because they add a new active signal rather than disabling an existing gate.

The Warburg effect — preferential aerobic glycolysis even in oxygen-replete conditions — follows directly from constitutive mTOR activation [7]. mTOR upregulates HIF-1α, which in turn upregulates GLUT1/GLUT3 glucose transporters and glycolytic enzymes. The lactate produced acidifies the tumor microenvironment, suppressing NK and T-cell function — a secondary mechanism by which metabolic reprogramming degrades immune surveillance. Cancer cachexia is partly the consequence of the tumor’s constitutive mTOR-driven biosynthesis competing for the same metabolic resources as normal tissue; inflammatory cytokines and proteolysis-inducing factor contribute additional arms.

## §3 — Metastasis as Second Independent Threshold

### §3.1 Two Independent Phase Transitions

Carcinogenesis and metastasis are distinct biological events separated in time by months to years. A cell can be malignant for years before acquiring metastatic competence because metastasis requires a second, independent threshold crossing — not a continuation of the first. The adhesion network governs positional integrity of cells in tissue. Cell-cell and cell-matrix adhesion molecules (E-cadherin, integrins, fibronectin, laminin) maintain a positional surveillance bound: cells in a non-native position are detected and eliminated. As long as the adhesion network is intact, cells remain in their home tissue.

### §3.2 Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transition as Adhesion Threshold Collapse

Epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) is the molecular program by which a cancer cell downregulates E-cadherin and upregulates mesenchymal markers (N-cadherin, vimentin, fibronectin). EMT is the adhesion surveillance collapse: E-cadherin loss removes the primary positional surveillance gate; integrin remodeling decreases the adhesion threshold further; below the positional threshold, the cell can invade neighboring tissue.

The literature has increasingly documented partial or hybrid EMT states — cells with intermediate adhesion phenotypes that may retain higher metastatic competence than fully mesenchymal cells. In the surveillance threshold framework, these represent intermediate positional threshold values: evidence that the threshold is a continuous parameter, not a binary switch. Partial EMT is consistent with this framework rather than a counterexample to it.

## §4 — Immunotherapy and the TMB Threshold

### §4.1 Immune Checkpoint Blockade as Activation Gate Restoration

PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint blockade (ICB) — pembrolizumab, nivolumab, atezolizumab — works by blocking the PD-1 inhibitory receptor on T cells. PD-L1 expressed by tumor cells binds PD-1 on tumor-infiltrating T cells, shutting down the T-cell response (closing the immune activation gate). ICB blocks this interaction, restoring T-cell activity [9].

ICB has a critical prerequisite: immune surveillance must have already detected the tumor. If T cells never recognized the tumor (no neoantigen recognition), restoring the gate does nothing — there is no recognition signal to act on. This is the hot/cold tumor dichotomy. Hot tumors have abundant neoantigens (high TMB), tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), and active anti-tumor immune infiltrate. Cold tumors have low TMB, sparse TILs, and predominantly immunosuppressive macrophage infiltrate.

The updated microglial/macrophage nomenclature is important here: the M1/M2 dichotomy is an oversimplification. Within the tumor microenvironment, TREM2-expressing tumor-associated macrophages (TREM2+ TAMs) are predominantly immunosuppressive — they suppress CD8+ T-cell activity, promote Treg induction, and are associated with poor ICB response and worse prognosis [34,35]. The TME context is the opposite of the brain literature where TREM2+ microglia are neuroprotective. TREM2 blockade enhances T-cell infiltration and augments ICB efficacy in preclinical models. NE/β-AR drives pro-tumorigenic macrophage polarization; ACh/α7-nAChR drives anti-inflammatory resolution. Throughout this paper, “DIM polarization” refers to monocyte-derived inflammatory macrophage phenotype — the pro-tumorigenic, NE/β-AR-driven state characterised by VEGF, MMP, and TNF-α secretion; distinct from TREM2+ TAMs but also immunosuppressive through different mechanisms.

### §4.2 Hot vs. Cold Tumors: The Neoantigen Recognition Threshold

Tumor mutational burden (TMB) quantifies the number of somatic mutations per megabase of tumor DNA. High TMB generates many neoantigens — peptides from mutant proteins presented on MHC-I. Immune surveillance activates when the cumulative neoantigen recognition signal crosses the immune activation threshold. For typical MHC-I binding energetics and T-cell repertoire diversity, this is consistent with approximately TMB_c ≈ 10 mut/Mb [10]. The threshold value varies by cancer type, assay platform, and patient population; the structural logic does not.

### §4.3 Immune Evasion as Mutation Rate Exceeding Adaptation Rate

Immune evasion occurs when the tumor mutation rate exceeds the immune system’s adaptation rate. As the tumor mutates rapidly, previously recognized neoantigens are lost (antigen loss variants), new neoantigens emerge faster than T-cell clones can expand to recognize them, and MHC-I expression may be downregulated directly. The structural asymmetry: tumor adaptive selection runs faster than the immune system’s adaptive response. Immune surveillance is always trailing tumor evolution.

## §5 — The Tumor–Brain Cascade

### §5.1 The Loop Nobody Owns

The tumor communicates with the immune system, which communicates with the brain, which modulates immune function, which affects the tumor. The loop runs continuously and bidirectionally [11,21,22]. Treating only one node in the loop treats one node. The cascade has two arms that were previously described as separate: a neuroinflammatory arm (cytokines → IDO → kynurenine → brain) and a neuroendocrine arm (stress → HPA → cortisol → immunosuppression → tumor). They are part of the same cascade.

### §5.2 The Cancer–Immune–Brain Cascade: IDO/Kynurenine Arm

The primary neuroinflammatory arm of the cascade runs through the IDO (indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase) enzyme, which is induced by tumor pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β) in both peripheral immune cells and in microglia once the cascade crosses the blood-brain barrier:

- Tumor → IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β (pro-inflammatory cytokines)

- Cytokines cross BBB via circumventricular organs or vagal C-fiber afferents → NTS → microglial activation

- Microglial IDO activation → tryptophan catabolism diverted from 5-HT synthesis to kynurenine pathway [23,24]

- Kynurenine → quinolinic acid (QUIN) via KMO enzyme → NMDA receptor agonist → hippocampal excitotoxicity

- Kynurenine → kynurenic acid (KYNA) via KAT enzyme → NMDA antagonist (neuroprotective arm)

- Net ratio determines hippocampal damage risk: sustained inflammation → KMO/QUIN dominates over KAT/KYNA

- Simultaneously: 5-HT synthesis rate ↓ (tryptophan diverted) → mood dysregulation, depression, fatigue, anhedonia

This cascade has two distinct neurotoxic arms: (1) direct NMDA excitotoxicity via QUIN, and (2) 5-HT depletion via tryptophan diversion. Previous descriptions of cancer-related cognitive impairment and depression focused on the 5-HT depletion arm. The QUIN/NMDA arm is mechanistically separate and more directly damaging to hippocampal architecture [41] — the same mechanism documented in treatment-resistant depression and severe sepsis. Standard serum serotonin or 5-HIAA measurement does not capture this arm; the kynurenine:tryptophan ratio or QUIN:KYNA ratio is the relevant biomarker.

### §5.3 The Reverse Loop: Brain → Tumor

The cascade also runs in reverse. Psychological stress → HPA axis activation → chronic cortisol elevation → immunosuppression → reduced NK cell and cytotoxic T-cell activity → reduced immune surveillance → tumor growth. This is a mechanistic cascade with identified molecular intermediaries:

- Chronic stress → CRH (PVN) → ACTH → cortisol (HPA cascade)

- Cortisol → GR on T cells → reduced IL-2 production, reduced T-cell proliferation

- Cortisol → NK cell cytotoxicity reduced (reduced perforin, granzyme B expression) [13,29,30]

- Cortisol → hippocampal GR → LTP suppression, DG neurogenesis ↓; chronic exposure → HPA brake failure (subiculum GR downregulation → cortisol runaway)

- Norepinephrine (sympathetic) → β-AR on tumor cells → VEGF upregulation → angiogenesis; on macrophages → DIM polarization → immunosuppressive TME [14]

The HPA brake failure loop is particularly relevant in cancer patients with chronic psychological stress or cancer-related HPA dysregulation: cortisol damages hippocampal architecture → HPA negative feedback weakens → more cortisol → more NK cell suppression → worse tumor surveillance. This is a positive feedback loop that no single intervention fully addresses without targeting the hippocampal health component.

### §5.4 Cancer-Related Fatigue: The Glymphatic-Adenosine Mechanism

Cancer-related fatigue (CRF) affects 70–80% of cancer patients and is multifactorial — cytokines, anemia, endocrine disruption, sleep disruption, deconditioning, and psychological factors all contribute [12]. The following is a mechanistically plausible hypothesis — the integrated cascade has not been directly demonstrated in cancer patients: tumor cytokines → microglial activation → NE_tonic elevation (from LC activation via IL-1β) → insufficient NE_tonic drop during sleep → glymphatic clearance impaired AND microglial synaptic pruning impaired simultaneously.

Both glymphatic clearance and microglial pruning require NE_tonic near zero during deep sleep. NE actively inhibits microglial motility [42]. When NE_tonic is chronically elevated (from tumor-driven LC activation, HPA dysregulation, or sleep-disrupting treatment), both maintenance operations are simultaneously impaired: adenosine accumulates (incomplete glymphatic clearance), synaptic clutter builds (insufficient pruning), and the activation ceiling drops — the physiological basis of the post-exertional pattern (disproportionate fatigue following activity with 24–48 hour recovery). This is mechanistically distinct from deconditioning and should be measured separately.

If this cascade operates as described, cancer patients should show elevated adenosine or adenosine metabolite levels in CSF or interstitial fluid correlating with fatigue severity and post-exertional decrement, with the correlation stronger for NE-pathway disruption markers than for inflammatory markers alone. This prediction is testable and has not been directly evaluated in cancer patients.

## §6 — Hormonal Architecture in Cancer

### §6.1 Estradiol and Hormone-Receptor-Positive Cancers

Approximately 70–75% of breast cancers express estrogen receptor alpha (ERα), making E2 the primary proliferative driver. The mechanism is direct: E2 binds ERα → nuclear translocation → activation of proliferative gene expression (cyclin D1, c-Myc, VEGF) → cell cycle entry. In this context ERα is a surveillance gate failure — what was a normally-gated signaling pathway for breast tissue becomes a constitutive proliferative signal when the estrogen environment is chronically elevated (post-menopausal exogenous estrogen, obesity-associated aromatase activity in adipose tissue).

Treatment architecture maps directly to the mechanism: tamoxifen (SERM, blocks ERα in breast tissue, reduces recurrence ~50% in ER-positive disease); aromatase inhibitors (letrozole, anastrozole, block peripheral E2 synthesis from adipose aromatase — first-line in postmenopausal ER-positive disease because they suppress the dominant E2 synthesis pathway); CDK4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib, ribociclib, target cyclin D1 downstream of ERα, substantially extending progression-free survival in combination with AIs [16]).

Endometrial cancer follows the same E2-driven mechanism. Unopposed estrogen drives endometrial hyperplasia → cancer. PTEN loss + E2-driven ERα activation = dual surveillance gate failure: PTEN loss removes PI3K/AKT suppression (constitutive mTOR) while E2-driven ERα provides positive proliferative drive. This explains why PTEN-null endometrial tumors are predominantly ER-positive — both gates must fail for malignancy to initiate.

### §6.2 Testosterone, DHT, and Prostate Cancer

Prostate cancer is androgen-dependent in its early and most treatable form. The androgen receptor (AR) governs prostate cell proliferation — testosterone and its more potent metabolite dihydrotestosterone (DHT, via 5α-reductase) bind AR → nuclear translocation → proliferative gene expression (PSA, TMPRSS2). Normal prostate homeostasis requires AR signaling; prostate cancer hijacks this homeostatic signal as its primary proliferative driver.

Treatment architecture: androgen deprivation therapy (ADT via GnRH agonists/antagonists, castrate testosterone \<50 ng/dL, ~95% initial response); second-generation anti-androgens (enzalutamide, apalutamide — direct AR block in castration-resistant prostate cancer) [17]; 5α-reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride, block DHT synthesis). Castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC) emerges through AR amplification, AR splice variants (AR-V7 — constitutively active without ligand), or intratumoral androgen synthesis — direct adaptive selection finding alternative routes to AR activation at castrate androgen levels.

### §6.3 Cortisol, HPA Axis, and the Immune Gate

Cortisol is a dual immune checkpoint suppressor — it reduces NK cell cytotoxicity (innate immune checkpoint) and cytotoxic T-cell activity (adaptive checkpoint) simultaneously. Chronic psychological stress, cancer-related sleep disruption, and treatment-induced HPA dysregulation all contribute to sustained cortisol elevation. The hippocampal brake failure loop (§5.3) means that cortisol-mediated hippocampal damage weakens the HPA negative feedback → more cortisol → more NK suppression — a self-reinforcing cascade that connects psychological distress directly to immune surveillance capacity.

The architecture predicts that cortisol measurement alongside tumor markers and immune infiltrate metrics [38] should improve prognostic stratification and ICB patient selection — a hypothesis that justifies prospective validation. Interventions with mechanistic cortisol-lowering evidence — sleep quality improvement, psychological support, mindfulness-based stress reduction, moderate exercise — are addressing a real immunological mechanism, not providing soft supportive care.

## §7 — Autonomic Neurochemical Signaling in the Tumor Microenvironment

### §7.1 The Sympathetic Axis: NE, β-Adrenergic Receptors, and Tumor Progression

Solid tumors receive direct sympathetic innervation — nerve terminals release NE and epinephrine into the tumor microenvironment. Tumor cells express functional β-1 and β-2 adrenergic receptors, and sympathetic activation has direct pro-tumorigenic effects [14]:

- NE → β-2-AR on tumor cells → cAMP → PKA → VEGF upregulation → angiogenesis

- NE → β-AR → PKA → MMP-2 and MMP-9 upregulation → extracellular matrix degradation → invasion and metastatic competence

- NE → β-AR → PKA → Bcl-2 upregulation → reduced anoikis → circulating tumor cell survival during metastatic transit (connects directly to the metastasis threshold, §3.2)

- NE → β-AR on macrophages → DIM polarization → tumor-promoting immunosuppressive microenvironment

The clinical evidence for beta-blocker use in cancer has strengthened beyond retrospective data. A phase II RCT (Haldar et al. 2020, n=60 breast cancer) showed preoperative propranolol downregulated mesenchymal gene expression and improved immune cell profile in primary tumors [36]. The COMPIT pilot RCT (Ricon-Becker et al. 2023, n=34 colorectal cancer) showed 5-year disease-free survival with 0/11 (0%) recurrence in the perioperative propranolol + etodolac arm vs 8/17 (47%) in placebo — the first prospective survival outcome data from a randomised trial [37]. The sample size is small and confidence intervals around these proportions are wide; this is proof-of-concept requiring confirmation in a powered Phase III trial. Larger retrospective cohorts remain heterogeneous.

### §7.2 The Parasympathetic Axis: Acetylcholine, Muscarinic Receptors, and Anti-Tumor Signaling

Vagal efferents reach most visceral organs and the tumor microenvironment. ACh released from parasympathetic terminals acts on muscarinic receptors (M1–M3) on tumor cells and immune cells with generally anti-tumorigenic effects. The cholinergic anti-inflammatory reflex [20,25,26] — vagal efferents → splenic nerve → α7-nAChR on macrophages → TNF-α suppression — is the brain’s primary real-time immune modulation pathway and is directly relevant to the tumor microenvironment: reduced TNF-α and IL-6 in the TME means reduced IDO activation, reduced kynurenine load, and reduced NF-κB-driven DIM macrophage polarization.

ACh → M1 muscarinic receptor on tumor cells → reduced Ras/MAPK signaling → reduced proliferation (notably lung, pancreatic cell lines). ACh → α7-nAChR on macrophages → anti-inflammatory phenotype. Autonomic dysfunction, common in cancer patients and worsened by treatment, shifts the balance toward sympathetic dominance. HRV measures this balance in real time: low HRV = sympathetic dominance = impaired cholinergic anti-inflammatory reflex = more permissive TME for tumor progression.

### §7.3 Substance P, NK1 Receptors, and Perineural Invasion

Note on aprepitant: a dosing gap must be resolved before any clinical trial design — most preclinical anti-tumour data were obtained at concentrations that may substantially exceed clinically achievable plasma levels with standard antiemetic dosing. The pharmacokinetic question of whether standard dosing produces sufficient NK1 occupancy in the tumour microenvironment is unanswered, and no robust human survival data exist. The mechanism is described here for completeness; it should not be interpreted as implying aprepitant is a viable repurposed candidate without pharmacokinetic validation.

Tumors actively recruit sensory nerve innervation — perineural invasion (PNI), the growth of tumor cells along nerve sheaths, is a well-established poor prognostic sign in pancreatic, prostate, colorectal, and head/neck cancers. Tumors secrete NGF and BDNF to recruit sensory and autonomic nerves; recruited nerves release Substance P (SP) that promotes tumor growth [18,19]:

- SP → NK1 receptor on tumor cells → Gq → IP3/DAG → PKC → PI3K/AKT activation → proliferation and survival

- SP → NK1 on tumor-associated macrophages → DIM polarization → immunosuppressive TME

- SP → NK1 on endothelial cells → VEGF upregulation → angiogenesis (parallel pathway to β-AR)

Aprepitant (fosaprepitant) is an NK1 receptor antagonist approved as an antiemetic for chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting. Preclinical models show anti-proliferative and pro-apoptotic activity via NK1 blockade [43].

## §8 — Surveillance-Derived Biomarkers for Early Detection and Monitoring

The surveillance architecture defined in this paper generates a specific set of measurable parameters that standard oncology currently does not monitor together — not because they are inaccessible, but because they belong to systems (endocrine, autonomic, neurological, circadian) that oncology has treated as outside its scope. If the architecture is correct, these parameters are directly relevant to cancer risk, detection, treatment selection, and recurrence prediction.

The panel logic: TMB tells you whether the immune gate has been triggered. Immune infiltrate tells you whether surveillance is actively operating in the tumor. Kynurenine:tryptophan ratio tells you whether IDO is suppressing that surveillance while simultaneously driving the brain cascade. Cortisol diurnal slope tells you whether the HPA arm is suppressing NK and CTL activity upstream of the tumor. HRV tells you whether sympathetic dominance is creating a pro-tumorigenic microenvironment in real time. None of these require new technology — they require ordering existing tests across specialties that currently do not communicate in oncology.

The circadian dimension is the most undercharacterized in current oncology. NK cell cytotoxicity follows a strong diurnal rhythm; overnight T-cell trafficking is melatonin-dependent. Cancer patients with untreated obstructive sleep apnea, chronic sleep fragmentation from HPA dysregulation, or shift-work history have a suppressed circadian immune amplitude — a nightly surveillance deficit that no current biomarker panel captures. Chronotherapy — timing immunotherapy to the circadian peak of immune effector activity — has growing preclinical support and is an open clinical gap. The circadian parameter also connects directly to the NE_tonic mechanism: tumor-driven LC activation elevates NE_tonic → delays melatonin onset → reduces overnight NK activation → reduces circadian surveillance amplitude. The tumor is suppressing its own overnight surveillance peak through the autonomic-pineal axis.

### §9.2 Testable Predictions

P1 and P6 follow directly from the surveillance threshold structure and would be less naturally generated by standard pathway reasoning alone. P2–P5 are mechanistically grounded by the cascade analysis and are consistent with the framework, though they could also be motivated by standard systems oncology reasoning independently. P7 is new to this version.

- P1 — TMB threshold: the threshold logic predicts a sharp inflection in ICB response probability near TMB_c, with near-zero response rates below it regardless of PD-L1 expression. Testable against large multi-cancer ICB datasets stratified by TMB.

- P2 — Stress biomarkers predict immune infiltrate: patients with high cortisol AUC and low HRV should have reduced tumor immune infiltrate (lower CD8+ T cell and NK cell density), controlling for treatment intensity.

- P3 — Beta-blocker survival signal: prospective randomized trials powered for survival endpoints in ER-positive breast cancer, prostate cancer, and melanoma would resolve the heterogeneous retrospective signal.

- P4 — Aprepitant tumor signal: stratified EHR analysis of cancer patients receiving aprepitant should control for treatment intensity and comorbidity before any survival signal can be attributed to NK1 blockade specifically. Pharmacokinetic gap must be resolved first.

- P5 — CRF glymphatic mechanism: cancer patients should show elevated CSF adenosine correlating with fatigue severity and post-exertional decrement; this correlation should be stronger for NE-pathway disruption markers than for inflammatory markers alone.

- P6 — Suppressor landscape predicts hit count: once individual barrier contributions are independently measured from binding energetics (the open gap in §10), this becomes a testable prediction — cancers with known suppressor landscapes should have N_hits consistent with the number of suppressors required to erode the quiescence barrier. Not testable in current form; conditional on closing the measurement gap first.

- P7 — Exercise and immune infiltrate: patients enrolled in structured exercise programs during treatment should show higher CD8+ T cell and NK cell tumor infiltrate, lower kynurenine:tryptophan ratio, lower cortisol AUC, and higher HRV compared to sedentary controls, with all four measured simultaneously in a single study.

## §10 — Open Gaps

- Independent measurement of individual suppressor barrier contributions from binding energetics and network topology. Currently estimated from known biology; quantitative measurement would allow forward prediction of N_hits rather than structural consistency check.

- Quantitative prediction of TMB_c from neoantigen recognition probability distributions. Requires peptide-MHC binding energy distributions across the tumor neoantigen landscape; the current threshold is empirically established, not forward-derived.

- Kynurenine:tryptophan ratio and QUIN:KYNA ratio as routine oncology biomarkers. These are not currently measured in standard care and would be needed to directly monitor the IDO/kynurenine arm of the tumor-brain cascade.

- CSF adenosine measurement in cancer patients: the glymphatic-adenosine model for CRF requires direct measurement to distinguish from confounded correlates, and to determine whether the NE_tonic arm (LC activation) or the inflammatory arm (microglial activation) drives adenosine accumulation.

- The four exercise-surveillance channels (NK mobilization, HPA/cortisol normalization, AMPK/mTOR competition, cholinergic reflex) have never been simultaneously measured with tumor immune infiltrate in a single human study. The quantitative contribution of each channel remains uncharacterized.

- Aprepitant pharmacokinetics in tumor microenvironment: NK1 occupancy at standard antiemetic dosing is uncharacterized. Preclinical anti-tumor concentrations may exceed clinically achievable levels; this gap must be resolved before prospective trial design.

- Explicit cascade modeling of the tumor-brain axis: at what tumor cytokine level does microglial IDO activation and QUIN production become self-sustaining after tumor removal? The threshold for neuroinflammation persistence is not characterized.

- Confounding in observational cascade data: stress, beta-blocker use, aprepitant exposure, and exercise all correlate with other clinical variables. Mendelian randomization for HPA axis variants, randomized beta-blocker trials, stratified aprepitant EHR analysis, and exercise RCTs powered for immune infiltrate endpoints are needed.
