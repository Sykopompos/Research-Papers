# Why Your Eosinophilic Esophagitis Won't Go Away — Plain Language Summary

*A plain language summary of "The Self-Sustaining Barrier: A Mechanistic Framework for Eosinophilic Esophagitis" (v0.9, March 2026). No medical background required.*

---

## What the paper is saying

Eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE) is a disease defined by a number: 15 white blood cells (eosinophils) per microscope view of your esophageal tissue. If you have that many, you have EoE. Treatment has spent twenty years focused on getting that number down.

This paper argues that the number is the wrong target.

The real problem is a damaged lining in the esophagus — the tube that runs from your mouth to your stomach — and the way that damage keeps making itself worse. Once you understand that mechanism, several things that currently make no sense about EoE start to click into place: why the obvious drug (dupilumab) works across all measures while the eosinophil-targeting drugs (despite reducing the eosinophil count dramatically) failed to help patients feel better; why milk is the most common trigger and removing it alone often works as well as removing six foods; and why EoE tends to come back when treatment stops.

---

## The esophagus is supposed to have a wall

The inner surface of your esophagus is lined with cells. Those cells are held together by protein locks called tight junctions, and they're coated with structural proteins including one called filaggrin. Together they form a barrier — a wall — between the inside of your esophagus and the immune cells sitting just underneath.

When the wall is healthy, food passes through without incident. Immune cells barely notice it.

In people with EoE — particularly those with certain genetic variants — this wall is weaker than normal from the start. The locks (tight junctions) are less secure. The structural coating (filaggrin) is thinner. When food antigens (typically proteins from cow's milk, eggs, wheat, or soy) pass through gaps in the wall and reach the immune cells underneath, the immune system reacts to them as if they were a threat.

---

## The alarm goes off

When the esophageal wall is breached, the lining cells release two chemical alarm signals called TSLP and IL-33. These aren't fighting the invaders directly — they're messengers that tell the immune system what kind of response to mount. In this case, they push the immune system toward a Th2 response: the kind of immune response associated with allergies and asthma.

The Th2 response produces several molecules, but the one that matters most here is called **IL-13**.

---

## IL-13 is the lock that breaks the key

IL-13 does three things simultaneously:

**1. It calls in eosinophils.** IL-13 causes the esophageal lining cells to produce a recruitment signal called eotaxin-3. Eotaxin-3 is what pulls eosinophils from the bloodstream into the esophageal tissue. This is where the diagnostic "number" comes from — it's counting the eosinophils that eotaxin-3 recruited.

**2. It drives scarring.** IL-13 activates the pathway that converts inflammation into fibrous tissue (collagen). This is how EoE progresses from a disease of inflammation (which is potentially reversible) to a disease of scarring with rings and strictures (which may require mechanical intervention).

**3. It breaks the wall it needs to fix.** IL-13 directly suppresses filaggrin and the tight junction proteins — the structural components of the barrier. The very inflammatory response that was triggered *by* the broken wall actively prevents the wall from repairing itself.

This is the self-sustaining loop:

> Broken wall → food antigens get through → immune alarm → IL-13 → eosinophils recruited + wall stays broken → more food antigens get through → more immune alarm → more IL-13

The loop doesn't automatically stop even if you remove the food trigger for a while. The wall can't repair itself while IL-13 is still suppressing the proteins it needs to rebuild. You need to interrupt the loop, not just remove the input.

---

## Why targeting eosinophils doesn't fix EoE

Several drugs target IL-5 — the molecule that keeps eosinophils alive and recruits them from bone marrow. These drugs (mepolizumab, reslizumab, benralizumab) reliably bring the eosinophil count down to near zero. In clinical trials, they achieved what looks like histologic remission by the diagnostic definition.

But patients didn't get better. Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing), food impaction (food getting stuck), and the endoscopic features of the disease — furrows, rings, exudates — didn't significantly improve.

The paper extends this with a second example. Barzolvolimab, a drug that depletes mast cells (a type of immune cell also involved in EoE), was tested in a Phase 2 trial in 2025. It depleted mast cells by 72%. Symptoms and endoscopic scores: unchanged.

The pattern is consistent: removing a downstream cellular player — whether eosinophils or mast cells — without interrupting IL-13 doesn't produce clinical benefit. The loop is still running.

---

## Why dupilumab works

Dupilumab doesn't target eosinophils or mast cells. It blocks the receptor that IL-13 (and IL-4) use to communicate. When IL-13 can't signal:

- Eotaxin-3 production falls → fewer eosinophils recruited
- Filaggrin suppression is relieved → wall can start to repair
- The fibrosis pathway is attenuated → less scarring

In the Phase 3 trial, 60% of patients achieved histologic remission. But more importantly, symptom scores improved significantly, endoscopic features improved, and patients actually felt better. This is what you'd expect if you interrupted the loop at the driver rather than cleaning up one of its outputs.

Cendakimab, a newer drug that targets IL-13 more directly (blocking the receptor itself rather than the shared receptor used by IL-13 and IL-4), showed 29.5% histologic response vs 2.1% placebo in its Phase 3 trial published in 2025 — with meaningful symptom improvement. A second confirmation that IL-13 is the right target.

---

## Why milk elimination works (and why six foods usually isn't necessary)

Most EoE patients are triggered by a small number of food proteins. The most common is cow's milk protein. When you remove milk from the diet, you remove the dominant food antigen that's penetrating the broken wall and triggering the IL-13 response. The loop loses its main fuel.

For most patients, that's enough.

A 2023 clinical trial compared milk-only elimination (remove one food) against six-food elimination (remove wheat, dairy, eggs, soy, nuts, and seafood) in 129 adult EoE patients. Remission rate with milk elimination: 34%. With six-food elimination: 40%. The difference was not statistically significant. A similar 2024 pediatric trial found comparable results.

The old approach was to start by removing all six foods simultaneously, then add them back one at a time to find the trigger. That's months of restricted eating and multiple endoscopies. The evidence now supports starting with milk alone, confirming response, and escalating only if milk removal doesn't work. For most patients, it does work.

## The diagnostic problem: we're testing too late

The standard EoE diagnosis requires 15 eosinophils per microscope view on biopsy. By the time you have that many eosinophils, the inflammatory cascade has been running for days to weeks. You're detecting the endpoint of a process, not the process itself.

Two earlier signals exist but aren't yet in routine use:

**Eotaxin-3** is a blood marker produced by IL-13-stimulated esophageal cells. It starts rising within 7–14 days of reintroducing a trigger food — before symptoms return and before eosinophil counts cross the diagnostic threshold. In theory, a blood draw at day 7 and day 14 after reintroducing a food could identify the trigger much faster than the current 6-week wait-and-biopsy approach.

**Mucosal impedance** is a test that measures the electrical resistance of the esophageal lining. Damaged tight junctions reduce resistance. It can detect barrier disruption before eosinophilia develops, and it can be done at the same time as the standard endoscopy. It's available at academic centres but not widely used.

The paper argues that these tools need validation and standardisation to allow disease monitoring without repeated biopsies.

---

## The scarring problem: time matters

EoE left untreated or inadequately treated can progress from inflammation to scar tissue. The inflammation phase is potentially reversible — treat the IL-13 loop, barrier repairs, eosinophilia resolves. The scar phase may require endoscopic dilation to open up narrowed segments, and whether it fully reverses even with good treatment is uncertain.

The key point: diagnostic delay correlates with more scarring. The longer it takes to diagnose EoE, the more likely the disease has progressed from something treatable to something that needs mechanical intervention as well. Getting diagnosed sooner matters.

## What this means if you have EoE

A few practical takeaways from the framework:

**On diet:** Start with milk elimination before trying six-food. The evidence supports it, it's much easier to maintain, and it works for most people. If milk doesn't work after 6 weeks and confirmed endoscopy, then escalate.

**On PPIs:** About 30–40% of EoE patients respond to proton pump inhibitors (acid-suppressing drugs). The paper proposes this is because acid and pepsin damage contributes to the barrier problem — fixing the acid problem may give the barrier enough stability to reduce IL-13 signalling. PPIs are a reasonable first step.

**On dupilumab vs older biologics:** If dietary therapy and PPIs don't produce lasting remission, the biologic data strongly favours IL-13-targeting approaches over eosinophil-targeting approaches. The latter reliably reduces the diagnostic number without reliably helping patients feel better.

**On monitoring:** If your doctor is using eosinophil count as the only measure of disease activity, the paper suggests that's incomplete — endoscopic scores, symptom scores, and (eventually) eotaxin-3 blood tests are more informative about whether the disease is actually under control.

## What the paper predicts and what could prove it wrong

The framework generates seven specific testable predictions. The most important ones:

1. **Eotaxin-3 on day 7–14 of food reintroduction** predicts whether that food will cause a relapse at week 6 biopsy. If this doesn't hold up in a prospective trial, the biomarker argument fails.

2. **Dupilumab should restore filaggrin and claudin expression more than mepolizumab.** If an RCT comparing the two finds they both equally normalise the barrier genes, the central claim of the paper is wrong.

3. **Mucosal impedance should normalise before eosinophil counts fall** in patients on dupilumab. If eosinophilia resolves first, barrier repair is not the primary mechanism.

4. **Tezepelumab** (a drug targeting the TSLP alarm signal upstream of IL-13) is currently in a Phase 3 trial. If it works as well as dupilumab, it confirms that TSLP is the critical upstream signal. If it works significantly worse, it suggests IL-13 is being produced via pathways that don't depend on TSLP — which would matter for treatment selection.

---

## Summary

EoE isn't primarily a disease of too many eosinophils. It's a disease of a broken esophageal wall that can't repair itself because the immune response to the broken wall produces IL-13, and IL-13 actively suppresses the proteins the wall needs to rebuild.

Removing eosinophils doesn't fix the wall. Removing the food trigger helps — particularly milk — but the wall may need the IL-13 signal switched off before it can fully recover.

That's why dupilumab works across all outcomes and the IL-5-targeting drugs don't. And it's why EoE tends to relapse when treatment stops: the constitutional barrier vulnerability is still there, and once the food antigen is reintroduced, the loop can restart.

The paper is a proposed framework, not a proven mechanism. But it organises the existing evidence in a way that the older eosinophil-centric model doesn't, and it generates specific falsifiable predictions. The trials that test those predictions will either confirm it or show where the model needs to be revised.
