## Abstract

Cancer recurrence is substantially mechanistically predictable. It follows from adaptive selection operating on failure cascades that current treatment addresses at one or two nodes while leaving the others intact. This paper maps each standard-of-care intervention against the full surveillance architecture established in Cancer as Surveillance Failure (v1.9) and identifies, for each intervention, the nodes left intact, the predictable failure mode, and what the architecture identifies as missing. Five upstream targets are characterised that current oncology does not manage as primary endpoints: the HPA/cortisol axis, IDO activation state (kynurenine:tryptophan ratio), sympathetic/parasympathetic balance (HRV), mTOR-driven metabolic competition in the tumor microenvironment, and sleep/circadian immune amplitude. A monitoring gap is defined: standard oncology measures downstream tumor output while the upstream parameters that determine whether immune surveillance can operate go untracked. Non-redundant combinations are identified where each component addresses what the other cannot. A cascade-complete intervention architecture is described requiring coordination across oncology, neuroendocrinology, autonomic medicine, sleep medicine, and exercise medicine. Seven prospective trial designs are derived directly from the architecture, each with specified endpoints, stratification criteria, and predicted subgroup effects.

**Keywords:** cancer recurrence; immune checkpoint blockade; IDO inhibition; HPA axis; sympathetic nervous system; HRV; circadian immunity; exercise oncology; systems oncology; treatment resistance

## Methods and Scope

This paper presents a novel analytical framework applied to cancer recurrence and treatment resistance. The central contribution is structural: a systematic mapping of each current standard-of-care intervention against a unified surveillance architecture, identifying for each the nodes left intact, the predictable failure mode, and the upstream parameters that current oncology does not manage as primary endpoints. Seven prospective trial designs are derived directly from this architecture, each with specified patient populations, endpoints, stratification criteria, feasibility tiers, and predicted subgroup effects. This is a companion paper to Cancer as Surveillance Failure (v1.9), which establishes the underlying architecture. Literature was identified through targeted searches of PubMed and Google Scholar focusing on: cancer recurrence mechanisms, beta-blocker and cancer outcomes, IDO inhibitor trials (ECHO-301/KEYNOTE-252), ICB resistance mechanisms, exercise and immune infiltrate, cortisol and cancer prognosis, OSA and cancer outcomes, and metformin and cancer survival. Evidence is categorised using the same three-tier system as the companion paper (Tier A: replicated, mechanistically confirmed; Tier B: mechanistically supported; Tier C: integrative synthesis). The cascade-complete architecture in §6 is a target map derived from established biology, not a treatment protocol. No new experimental data are reported. This paper is written in the first person throughout; the use of ‘we’ in any quoted literature reflects the original source.

## §1 — Recurrence Is Predictable: Adaptive Selection on an Incompletely Corrected System

Much of cancer recurrence is substantially mechanistically predictable — not simply a matter of chance. It follows as a predictable outcome of adaptive selection operating on a failure cascade that was addressed at one or two nodes while the others remained intact. When oncology removes a tumor, kills dividing cells, blocks an oncogene, or restores an immune checkpoint, it does not restore the surveillance architecture. It removes the most visible expression of the failure while leaving the permissive conditions in place. Tumor evolution continues under the new selection pressure, and the clone that survives the intervention is by definition the one best adapted to evade whatever was applied.

The recurrence timeline encodes the mechanism. Hormone-receptor-positive breast cancer recurring 10–15 years after adjuvant endocrine therapy is not a new cancer — it is a dormant clone that survived under anti-estrogen pressure and re-emerged when surveillance conditions permitted [1]. Castration-resistant prostate cancer emerging within 18–24 months of ADT monotherapy is not treatment failure in the conventional sense — it is AR amplification and AR-V7 splice variant selection under androgen deprivation pressure, exactly as the mechanism predicts [2]. MSI-stable colorectal cancer failing ICB is not ICB failure — it is the structural consequence of a TMB below the recognition threshold; the surveillance gate was never triggered; restoring it did nothing [3].

Each recurrence pattern has a mechanistic reading. The reading requires knowing the full architecture, not just the node that was treated.

This framing is compatible with, and complementary to, the clonal evolution literature. McGranahan and Swanton [21] and the TRACERx consortium [22] have established that tumors are genetically heterogeneous, evolving under Darwinian selection pressure, with branched clonal architecture generating subpopulations with distinct mutational profiles. Under that framework, recurrence reflects the outgrowth of pre-existing resistant subclones that survived treatment. The cascade architecture presented here operates one level upstream: it describes the system-level permissive conditions — immune surveillance capacity, HPA state, IDO activation, sympathetic dominance, metabolic TME — that determine which clones face meaningful selection pressure and which survive in an environment that allows continued proliferation. Clonal heterogeneity explains the molecular identity of the recurrent clone; the cascade architecture explains why the environment remained permissive for it to emerge. Both are necessary; neither is complete without the other.

## §3 — The Upstream Targets Nobody Manages as Primary

The companion paper (§5–7) established that five system-level parameters directly determine immune surveillance capacity in the tumor microenvironment and in the brain: the HPA/cortisol axis, IDO activation state, sympathetic/parasympathetic balance, mTOR metabolic competition, and sleep/circadian immune amplitude. None of these are managed as primary oncology targets. All of them are managed — if at all — as secondary concerns by specialists who do not communicate with the treating oncologist and who are not using these parameters as oncology endpoints.

### §3.1 The HPA/Cortisol Axis

Cortisol is a dual immune checkpoint suppressor. It suppresses NK cell cytotoxicity (perforin and granzyme B expression reduced), cytotoxic T-cell activity (IL-2 production and proliferation reduced), and antigen presentation (dendritic cell maturation impaired) simultaneously via glucocorticoid receptor signaling [12,26]. These are the same effectors that ICB is trying to activate. A patient with chronically elevated cortisol receiving ICB is operating against the cortisol-mediated suppression simultaneously — the gate has been nominally restored but a separate suppressive force is holding it partially closed.

The HPA brake failure loop (established in §5.3 of the companion paper) makes this self-reinforcing: chronic cortisol → hippocampal damage → subiculum GR downregulation → HPA negative feedback weakens → more cortisol. This loop may run independently of tumor biology once established — the components are characterised individually, but whether tumour removal interrupts it in cancer patients has not been directly demonstrated. Tier B inference from mechanism; downgraded from a definitive claim. Hippocampal health support via exercise-driven DG neurogenesis [4] is mechanistically indicated but not yet formally tested in oncology as a primary endpoint.

Clinical consequence: the architecture predicts that cortisol diurnal slope assessment before ICB initiation should improve response rates — a prospectively testable hypothesis. Patients with a flat diurnal slope (blunted morning peak, elevated evening cortisol) have structurally impaired immune surveillance capacity regardless of their TMB or PD-L1 status. ICB in that context is restoring a gate that is being held closed by a mechanism the treatment does not address.

### §3.2 IDO Activation State

IDO (indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase) is one of the most potent immune checkpoint mechanisms in the tumor microenvironment — and currently one of the most underutilized targets in the clinic. Tumor cells, tumor-associated macrophages, and dendritic cells under inflammatory cytokine stimulation all upregulate IDO1, which converts tryptophan to kynurenine. This depletes tryptophan — which T cells require for proliferation — and produces kynurenine, which directly suppresses T-cell activation and promotes Treg induction [11]. The result: T cells in an IDO-high TME cannot respond to neoantigens even if they can recognize them.

IDO inhibition (epacadostat) failed in a Phase III trial in combination with pembrolizumab (ECHO-301/KEYNOTE-252) [5]. The failure was widely interpreted as IDO not being a valid target. The architectural reading is different: the trial was not stratified by IDO activation state (kynurenine:tryptophan ratio), which means it combined IDO-high and IDO-low patients indiscriminately. IDO inhibition is only mechanistically relevant in IDO-high tumors. This is the same category error as giving ICB without TMB stratification — treating all patients as if they share the same failure mode. Alternative explanations for the ECHO-301 failure deserve acknowledgment: epacadostat had known pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic limitations, with evidence of inadequate target coverage in vivo at the doses used; there may have been off-target immunosuppressive effects from IDO inhibition on regulatory T cells; and the pembrolizumab arm overperformed historical expectations, potentially obscuring modest additive effects. The K:T stratification hypothesis is the most parsimonious explanation if IDO is a genuine tumour-immune evasion mechanism, but it remains unconfirmed in the absence of the K:T subgroup analysis from ECHO-301 data.

The IDO/kynurenine pathway also drives the brain cascade (QUIN-mediated NMDA excitotoxicity and 5-HT depletion, companion paper §5.2). K:T ratio monitoring thus serves two purposes simultaneously: it tracks TME immunosuppression and brain cascade risk in a single measurement.

### §3.3 Sympathetic/Parasympathetic Balance

The sympathetic-parasympathetic balance in the tumor microenvironment determines macrophage polarization, NK cell activity, and angiogenic signaling simultaneously. NE/β-AR drives DIM (pro-tumorigenic) macrophage polarization, VEGF upregulation, and Bcl-2-mediated anoikis resistance [10]. ACh/α7-nAChR drives the cholinergic anti-inflammatory reflex, suppresses TNF-α and IDO activation, and supports anti-tumor macrophage phenotypes. HRV is the non-invasive real-time proxy for this balance.

No oncology protocol currently monitors HRV as a treatment parameter. No oncology protocol adjusts treatment sequencing based on autonomic balance. The beta-blocker data (heterogeneous retrospective) is the closest the field has come to engaging with this axis, and it is not yet at prospective trial stage. Meanwhile, the mechanism is well-characterized and the measurement tool (HRV) is cheaper and less invasive than any biomarker currently used in routine oncology monitoring.

### §3.4 mTOR Metabolic Competition

The Warburg effect is not simply a metabolic curiosity — it is an immune evasion mechanism. Constitutively active mTOR in tumor cells drives high-rate aerobic glycolysis [14,15], producing a lactate-acidified microenvironment. NK cells and T cells are functionally impaired at low pH; their glycolytic metabolism is outcompeted by the tumor’s constitutive mTOR-driven glucose consumption. The result is a metabolically hostile zone that suppresses the very effectors immune surveillance requires.

AMPK activation (via exercise, metformin, or caloric restriction) suppresses mTOR and reverses the metabolic competition asymmetry [13]. Metformin — already in oncology use for its AMPK activation in diabetic cancer patients — has shown survival associations in several cancer types [6], though the mechanistic attribution remains contested. The architecture suggests the relevant pathway is not simply glucose lowering but AMPK-mediated mTOR suppression reducing the metabolic advantage of tumor cells in the TME. This is testable by measuring TME pH, NK cytotoxicity, and T-cell metabolic fitness in metformin vs. non-metformin patients with matched disease characteristics.

### §3.5 Sleep and Circadian Immune Amplitude

The circadian immune amplitude — the day/night oscillation in NK cytotoxicity and T-cell trafficking — is the most completely ignored axis in current oncology. NK cell cytotoxicity peaks during the day; T-cell trafficking and proliferation peak overnight under melatonin entrainment [24,25]. Cancer patients with untreated obstructive sleep apnea, chronic sleep fragmentation from HPA dysregulation, or shift-work history are losing a nightly surveillance peak that is currently invisible to standard monitoring [16].

The NE_tonic mechanism connects this directly to the tumor: tumor-driven LC activation (via IL-1β and CRH) elevates NE_tonic → delays melatonin onset → reduces overnight NK activation → reduces circadian surveillance amplitude. The tumor is actively suppressing its own overnight surveillance through the autonomic-pineal axis — and no oncology protocol screens for this, monitors it, or treats it as a primary endpoint.

Chronotherapy — timing immune-activating interventions (ICB administration, exercise, NK-stimulating agents) to the circadian peak of effector activity — has preclinical support and is an open clinical gap. The architecture predicts that treating OSA in cancer patients would improve immune surveillance capacity and justifies prospective evaluation as an oncological co-intervention rather than solely a quality-of-life measure.

## §4 — The Monitoring Gap: Measuring Downstream While the Upstream Goes Untracked

Standard oncology monitoring tracks tumor response (imaging, tumor markers) and treatment toxicity (blood counts, organ function panels). It does not track the upstream system parameters that determine whether immune surveillance is operating, whether the TME is permissive or hostile, or whether the cascade conditions that permitted initiation have been corrected. The monitoring architecture is inverted: it is measuring the output of the failure cascade while the inputs go untracked.

## §5 — Non-Redundant Combination Logic

The most important combinations in oncology are not those that add more of the same mechanism — they are those that address different nodes of the same cascade. The distinction is between additive combinations (two drugs hitting the same pathway — often just more toxicity) and non-redundant combinations (two interventions addressing different cascade nodes, each of which is necessary for the other to work optimally). The architecture identifies the non-redundant combinations directly.

## §6 — What Cascade-Complete Intervention Requires

A treatment program that addresses the full failure cascade requires coordinating five specialties around a shared target map. Currently those specialties manage their domains in isolation: oncology manages the tumor; psychiatry or psychology manages psychological distress; endocrinology manages hormonal consequences of treatment; sleep medicine manages obstructive sleep apnea (if the patient is referred); physiotherapy prescribes exercise (if the patient is referred). Nobody is managing the cascade.

The cascade-complete target map is not a new therapy. Every intervention it requires already exists. What does not exist is the architecture for deploying them together, in sequence, with shared monitoring endpoints. The figure below is that architecture.

### §6.1 Implementation Barriers

The cascade-complete architecture described above does not currently exist for structural reasons beyond institutional inertia. Several barriers are worth naming explicitly. First, reimbursement: none of the upstream interventions — cortisol monitoring, HRV tracking, IDO inhibition, structured exercise as immune support — are reimbursed as oncology endpoints; they sit in separate billing categories across separate specialties. Second, trial infrastructure: multi-modal lifestyle and pharmacological combinations are difficult to randomise and power; FDA and EMA clearance pathways are designed for single-agent or fixed-combination drugs, not for “prescription exercise plus cortisol monitoring plus beta-blocker plus ICB.” Third, pharmaceutical incentives: off-patent drugs like propranolol and metformin, which feature prominently in the non-redundant combination logic, generate no commercial development incentive. The Ricon-Becker COMPIT trial was explicitly noted by its authors to face this barrier. None of these are arguments against the architecture — they are the specific infrastructure problems that would need to be solved to test it. Academic medical centres with integrated supportive care programmes are the most likely early implementation sites.

### §7.2 Open Mechanistic Gaps

- The quantitative weight of each exercise channel (NK mobilization, HPA normalization, AMPK/mTOR, cholinergic reflex) on immune infiltrate outcome is not characterized. Exercise dose-response for each channel independently is unknown.

- The K:T threshold for IDO-driven ICB resistance has not been formally established. The ECHO-301 K:T subgroup data has not been published; that analysis would substantially clarify whether K:T stratification rescues IDO inhibitor efficacy.

- Circadian immune amplitude measurement has no standardized clinical protocol. The relevant assay (NK cytotoxicity at circadian peak vs. nadir) is not part of any clinical workflow.

- The NE_tonic / melatonin / NK activation chain in cancer patients specifically has not been measured as a connected cascade. Each link has been studied independently; the quantitative cascade from tumor-driven LC activation to NK suppression has not been traced in human subjects.

- The perioperative NE spike magnitude and its effect on CTC survival is documented in animal models [9]; prospective human measurement in the perioperative window with concurrent CTC quantification has not been done.

- Cascade-complete vs. standard treatment: no trial has enrolled patients to receive the full upstream intervention package (exercise + cortisol monitoring + HRV-guided autonomic intervention + OSA treatment + metabolic monitoring) alongside standard oncology, powered for survival outcomes.
