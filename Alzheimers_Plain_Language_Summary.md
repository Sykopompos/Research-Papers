# Alzheimer's Disease: Plain Language Summary

*A companion to the technical paper "Alzheimer's Disease: Glymphatic Clearance Failure, Multi-System Convergence, and Peripheral System Drivers of Neurodegeneration" (v1.13, March 2026)*

*M. Finnegan · Preprint · Not yet peer reviewed*

---

## What this paper is about

Most people think of Alzheimer's as a brain disease — something that goes wrong inside your head. This paper argues that's only half the picture. The brain doesn't fail in isolation. It relies on the rest of your body staying healthy to keep itself clean, protected, and structurally intact. When those body systems start failing — your heart, your metabolism, your gut, your hormones, your sleep — the brain loses its ability to clear the waste it produces every day. That's when Alzheimer's begins.

The central idea is this: **Alzheimer's is a waste-disposal failure.** The brain produces a protein called amyloid-beta constantly, every day of your life. Normally, while you sleep, a system in your brain washes that waste away. When the washing system is disrupted — from poor sleep, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, hormonal changes, lack of exercise, or gut inflammation — amyloid starts building up. Once enough accumulates, it triggers a chain reaction that destroys neurons over the following years to decades.

---

## The brain's garbage truck: the glymphatic system

The most important system this paper describes is the **glymphatic system** — the brain's waste clearance mechanism. It works almost exclusively during deep sleep. Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flows through channels alongside your brain's blood vessels, picking up waste products including amyloid, and flushing them out. Think of it as a nightly power wash.

The pump for this system turns out to be a small region in your brainstem called the **locus coeruleus (LC)**. During deep sleep, LC neurons pulse slowly — about once every 50 seconds — and those pulses drive rhythmic contractions in the brain's blood vessels, which powers the CSF flow. A 2025 study confirmed this mechanism in rodents; human confirmation is still in progress.

The critical insight: **the brain needs quality deep sleep to clear its own waste.** Disrupted sleep doesn't just leave you tired. Over years and decades, it accumulates a clearance deficit that progressively moves the brain closer to the threshold where amyloid starts building up irreversibly.

---

## Why your whole body matters

The paper maps seven body systems that each directly affect whether the brain can clear amyloid:

**Sleep** — the most direct. Poor sleep directly impairs the glymphatic system. Even one night of total sleep deprivation measurably increases amyloid in spinal fluid in healthy people.

**Metabolism and diabetes** — Type 2 diabetes approximately doubles Alzheimer's risk. Insulin resistance in the brain impairs memory circuits directly, while high blood sugar activates proteins that increase amyloid production. The mechanism is direct, not just correlational.

**Heart and blood vessels** — High blood pressure, atherosclerosis, and cardiovascular disease damage the blood vessels in the brain. This reduces blood flow, breaks down the blood-brain barrier, and directly impairs the glymphatic channels that carry waste away.

**Hormones** — Estradiol (the main estrogen) protects the glymphatic system and suppresses amyloid production. Its loss at menopause simultaneously increases production and decreases clearance. This is a leading mechanistic explanation for why women have higher Alzheimer's rates than men after age 75.

**Gut microbiome** — An imbalanced gut microbiome increases systemic inflammation, which reaches the brain and activates the brain's immune cells. Those activated immune cells (microglia) release chemicals that increase amyloid production. The gut connection is real but the causal chain in humans is still being established.

**Exercise** — Physical exercise protects the brain through four independent pathways simultaneously: it releases growth factors (BDNF, irisin, osteocalcin) that maintain neurons, reduces inflammation, improves cardiovascular health for better brain blood flow, and produces lactate that directly signals the brain to grow new blood vessels.

**Stress hormones** — Chronic high cortisol activates the same protein that produces amyloid (BACE1), while simultaneously suppressing the immune cells that would clear it. The stress-Alzheimer's link is mechanistically coherent, though the chain specifically in humans needs more confirmation.

## The tipping point model

The paper frames Alzheimer's as a **threshold problem**. Your brain is always producing amyloid and always clearing it. As long as clearance keeps up with production, you're fine. When production starts chronically exceeding clearance — when the balance tips — the aggregation cascade begins. The bad news: this tipping point is usually crossed 20–30 years before any memory symptoms appear. The good news: all the body systems above are modifiable, and addressing them shifts the balance back toward clearance.

This means Alzheimer's prevention isn't primarily about any single drug. It's about keeping all the systems that maintain brain waste clearance functioning well across your lifetime.

---

## How the disease actually destroys memory

Once amyloid builds up, it triggers a second protein — **tau** — to go wrong. Tau normally holds neurons' internal skeleton together. When it misfolds, it spreads through the brain in a predictable pattern (called Braak staging), destroying neurons as it goes. Memory starts failing when tau reaches the hippocampus — the brain's new-memory filing system. Remote memories survive longer because they're stored in the cortex and don't need the hippocampus to be retrieved. This is why someone with Alzheimer's can vividly recall their wedding from 50 years ago but can't remember what they had for breakfast.

The locus coeruleus — the same region that drives the glymphatic pump — is the first place tau appears, often decades before any symptoms. This is why early loss of smell (the LC connects to the olfactory bulb) may be an early warning sign.

## Diagnosis: where things stand in 2026

A major recent advance is that **Alzheimer's can now be diagnosed with a blood test**. The FDA cleared the first blood-based Alzheimer's diagnostic in May 2025 — a test measuring two proteins (p-tau217 and amyloid-beta) that reliably predicts whether amyloid plaques are present in the brain. This replaces or supplements what previously required either a spinal tap or an expensive PET scan.

Researchers are also investigating whether simple smell tests might detect the earliest Braak stage tau changes — potentially identifying risk years before any other biomarker turns positive.

## Treatments: what's approved, what's coming

**Currently approved:**

- **Lecanemab (Leqembi)** and **donanemab (Kisunla)** — antibodies that clear amyloid from the brain. They slow cognitive decline by approximately 27–35% in early-stage patients. They don't stop the disease, but they meaningfully slow it. They work by targeting the most toxic early forms of amyloid before they form plaques.
- **Cholinesterase inhibitors** (donepezil, rivastigmine) — older drugs that compensate for neurotransmitter depletion. Symptom management only, no effect on disease progression.

**In development:**

- **Tau-targeting therapies** — drugs that reduce tau production (BIIB080, a gene-silencing approach) or clear tau aggregates (bepranemab). In Phase 2 with promising early signals. The rationale: amyloid initiates the disease but tau kills the neurons, so targeting tau may be necessary for meaningful cognitive protection.
- **Combination approaches** — anti-amyloid plus anti-tau, initiated before symptoms appear. No completed RCT yet, but mechanistically motivated.

**What didn't work:**

- Semaglutide (EVOKE/EVOKE+ trials, 2025) — the diabetes drug failed its primary cognitive endpoint in a large Phase 3 trial in early-stage AD patients. Biomarker improvements were seen, but no cognitive benefit. The paper argues this may reflect the drug being tested too late, in patients whose disease is already self-sustaining.

---

## The big picture

This paper is arguing that Alzheimer's has been misframed as purely a brain disease that you either get or don't. The evidence increasingly supports a different view: **it's a failure of the whole-body system that keeps the brain clean**, playing out over decades, and substantially shaped by factors that are modifiable long before any symptoms appear.

The drugs now being approved clear amyloid from a brain whose clearance system is already broken. The next frontier — and the argument of this paper — is identifying and correcting the upstream failures that allowed the amyloid to accumulate in the first place.
