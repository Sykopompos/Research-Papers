# Why Your Reflux Might Be the Wrong Disease — Plain Language Summary

*A plain language summary of "Sphincter Gate Failure and the Pepsin Problem: A Mechanistic Framework for Distinguishing GERD and LPR" (v2.2, March 2026). No medical background required.*

---

## What the paper is saying

Millions of people are being treated for the wrong disease.

GERD (gastroesophageal reflux disease) and LPR (laryngopharyngeal reflux, also called silent reflux) are currently treated as the same condition — both get proton pump inhibitors (PPIs, the acid-suppressing drugs like omeprazole or lansoprazole). For GERD, this works well. For LPR, it frequently doesn't — and the reason why tells you something important about what's actually causing the damage.

The paper argues these are two structurally distinct conditions that happen to share a common starting point. Getting the distinction right changes everything: diagnosis, diet, and treatment.

---

## The two gates

Your esophagus — the tube from your mouth to your stomach — has two valves, or "gates," that are supposed to keep stomach contents from flowing back up.

**Gate 1: The lower esophageal sphincter (LES).** This is where the esophagus meets the stomach. It's supposed to stay closed except when you swallow. When it fails — or opens at the wrong times — stomach contents can reflux into the esophagus. This is GERD.

**Gate 2: The upper esophageal sphincter (UES).** This is higher up, where the esophagus meets the throat. It's the backup gate. If refluxate gets past Gate 1, Gate 2 is supposed to catch it before it reaches the larynx (voice box), pharynx (throat), nose, ears, and airway.

**GERD is Gate 1 failing.** Acid gets into the esophagus, damages it, causes heartburn.

**LPR is Gate 1 AND Gate 2 failing.** Refluxate reaches the larynx, throat, and sometimes the ears and lungs. And the thing doing the damage isn't acid — it's pepsin.

---

## The pepsin problem

Pepsin is a digestive enzyme your stomach uses to break down protein. It's perfectly harmless in the stomach. The problem is what it does when it escapes.

Unlike acid — which is instantly obvious because it burns — pepsin is subtle and persistent:

**1. Pepsin doesn't wash off.** When pepsin reaches the larynx, it binds to laryngeal cells and gets pulled inside them. It stays there for hours after the reflux event has resolved. You can feel fine and have active pepsin sitting in your laryngeal tissue from an episode that happened at 2am.

**2. Pepsin reactivates on dietary acid.** Even when pepsin is "dormant" at neutral pH, it wakes up whenever it encounters anything acidic — citrus, carbonated water, coffee, tomato products, vinegar. The CO₂ in a sip of sparkling water drops your throat's pH enough to reactivate pepsin that refluxed hours earlier. There was no new reflux event. The damage is happening from yesterday's pepsin meeting today's orange juice.

**3. PPIs don't touch pepsin.** Proton pump inhibitors work by blocking acid production. They do nothing to pepsin secretion, nothing to pepsin that's already in the larynx, and nothing to stop the reactivation cycle. This is why LPR patients on twice-daily PPI often don't get better — the treatment isn't addressing the thing causing the damage.

There's one more twist: PPIs can actually make LPR worse. Normal solid food digestion requires pepsin in the stomach to break down food. Suppress acid, deactivate pepsin, and food takes longer to empty from the stomach. Longer stomach distension → more reflux events → more pepsin reaching the larynx. Some patients placed on PPI experience more throat symptoms, not fewer. This isn't the PPI failing — it's the PPI working exactly as intended, but in a way that makes the primary problem worse.

## What LPR actually feels like

LPR is frequently misdiagnosed because it doesn't feel like heartburn. The average LPR patient waits 4–8 years before getting a correct diagnosis.

Typical symptoms:
- Chronic throat clearing
- Hoarseness, especially in the morning
- Post-nasal drip (the constant feeling of something dripping down the back of the throat)
- Globus sensation (feeling of a lump in the throat)
- Chronic cough that won't respond to asthma treatment
- Recurrent ear fullness or Eustachian tube problems
- Often, **no heartburn at all**

That last point is why it gets missed. The patient presents with throat symptoms, gets sent to an ENT, gets told it's post-nasal drip, gets antihistamines and nasal sprays, gets no better. Or they get a reflux diagnosis and get PPIs. PPIs don't help. They get labelled "treatment-resistant" or told the symptoms are psychosomatic.

The paper argues most of those "treatment-resistant reflux" patients have LPR — a pepsin disease being treated with an acid-suppression drug.

## The treatment framework the paper proposes

The paper is explicit that this is a mechanistic framework, not a clinical guideline. But the logic follows directly from the biology.

**For GERD (acid-dominant):**
The standard approach is right. PPIs address the primary damaging agent. Lifestyle changes that reduce reflux events (smaller meals, no late eating, left-side sleeping) reduce how often acid reaches the esophagus.

**For pepsin-dominant LPR, the treatment priorities are different:**

*Step 1: Stop reactivating the pepsin you already have.*
Eliminate all dietary acid — not because it's causing reflux, but because it's waking up bound laryngeal pepsin. Citrus, carbonated drinks (including sparkling water), vinegar, tomato products, coffee. This is the intervention most LPR patients aren't doing because nobody explained why.

*Step 2: Inactivate the pepsin directly.*
Alkaline water (pH ≥ 8.0) permanently denatures pepsin — it changes the protein structure so it can't refold into an active enzyme. Sipping alkaline water throughout the day, especially after meals and before sleep, attacks the pepsin directly. This is different from antacids, which briefly neutralise acid. The alkaline water is targeting the enzyme.

Alginate preparations create a physical raft that floats on top of stomach contents and blocks refluxate from reaching the upper sphincter. These have been shown non-inferior to PPIs for LPR in 3 of 4 controlled studies. **Important:** The product that works is **Gaviscon Advance** — the UK formulation (sodium alginate 500mg per dose). The US version of Gaviscon is a completely different product (aluminium/magnesium antacid) and does not have the same mechanism or evidence. Reckitt, the manufacturer, has simply never introduced the high-alginate formula to the US market — they positioned US Gaviscon as a traditional antacid instead. UK Gaviscon Advance is not on US pharmacy shelves, but it is available through Amazon.com and specialty import pharmacies, typically at a higher price. Alternatively, **Reflux Gourmet** is a US-available sodium alginate product with the same raft-forming mechanism. Buying standard US Gaviscon off a domestic shelf expecting it to work like UK Gaviscon Advance is one of the most common and frustrating mistakes LPR patients make — the name is the same, the product is not.

*Step 3: PPI, twice daily.*
PPIs still have a role — keeping gastric pH higher reduces the chance of pepsinogen activating in the first place. But they work differently in LPR than GERD and must be combined with the pepsin-specific steps above.

**For patients with symptoms that don't respond to any of the above:**
Some reflux patients have what's called central sensitisation — the nervous system has become hypersensitised, so stimuli that shouldn't cause symptoms (mild distension, temperature changes) trigger pain or discomfort anyway. These patients won't respond to PPIs because the problem isn't in the stomach or larynx — it's in the nervous system's processing of signals. Low-dose antidepressants (not for depression, but because they modulate pain pathways) and specific anticonvulsant drugs (pregabalin) are the mechanistically appropriate treatments.

## Why the distinction matters so much

A patient with LPR who is:
- On twice-daily PPI (acid suppressed, pepsin intact)
- Drinking coffee and sparkling water (reactivating laryngeal pepsin constantly)
- Not using alkaline water
- Labelled "treatment-resistant"

...is not treatment-resistant. They are being treated for the wrong thing with the wrong drug.

The paper estimates this describes a substantial fraction of the millions of people currently labelled "PPI-refractory GERD." These aren't pharmaceutical failures. They're mechanism mismatches.

## Seven predictions

The paper derives seven specific predictions from the framework — testable things that should be true if the model is correct. The most immediately actionable:

**P1:** A simple salivary test for pepsin (already commercially available) should predict which reflux patients have LPR vs GERD and which will respond to PPIs alone vs need pepsin-specific treatment. No expensive imaging required.

**P2:** Adding alkaline water and dietary acid elimination to PPI therapy should produce better symptom scores in confirmed LPR patients than PPI alone — specifically in pepsin-positive patients.

**P3:** Patients on GLP-1 agonist drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro) who develop new reflux symptoms should be showing increased reflux event frequency (not just more acid), which would confirm the gastric emptying mechanism.

These are trials that haven't been run yet. This paper is designed to generate them.

---

## The bigger picture

The field of gastroenterology and ENT has been treating reflux as one disease because the treatments sit in one specialty (gastroenterology) and the symptoms present to a different specialty (ENT, pulmonology). The pepsin mechanism — established in the primary literature since the early 2000s — hasn't been translated into standard management protocols.

The patients falling through the gap between those specialties are the 40–50% of reflux patients whose symptoms don't adequately respond to standard PPI therapy. This paper's argument is that the non-response is predictable — it was always predictable — because the treatment was never addressing the primary damaging agent in their disease.
