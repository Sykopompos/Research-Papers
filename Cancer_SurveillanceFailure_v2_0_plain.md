# Cancer as Surveillance Failure — Plain Language Summary

*A plain language summary of the paper "Cancer as Surveillance Failure" (v2.0, March 2026). No medical background required.*

---

## What the paper is saying

Your body already has a system designed to catch cancer. Multiple systems, actually. The argument in this paper is that cancer — all cancer — is what happens when enough of those systems fail at the same time. And that oncology, as a field, has been treating individual nodes of a much larger interconnected failure while leaving most of it unaddressed.

That's not a critique of oncologists. It's a structural problem. Oncology, immunology, neurology, and endocrinology developed as separate specialties, and they study the same body through separate windows. The body doesn't know that. It runs as one integrated system, and cancer exploits the gaps between the windows.

---

## The security system analogy

Think of your body as a building with multiple independent security systems — each one asking the same basic question about every cell: *does this cell belong here, doing this, at this rate?*

**Layer 1: Tumor suppressors.** These are molecular checkpoints inside cells. When a cell's DNA gets damaged, tumor suppressors step in to either repair it, pause the cell, or trigger it to self-destruct. p53 is the most well-known — it's often called the "guardian of the genome." Losing p53 is like disabling the smoke detector. The fire can still start.

You have about five or six of these checkpoints working in redundancy. (The mutation count — five to seven hits — doesn't map exactly one-to-one with the checkpoint count, because some checkpoints require two hits to fully disable and because the immune system itself functions as an additional external layer.) Cancer doesn't start by disabling one of them. It needs to disable most of them, in the right order. That's why cancer is rare given the number of cells dividing in your body every day. Each individual failure can be caught by the others. Only when the accumulation crosses a threshold does the cell finally start dividing uncontrollably.

**Layer 2: The immune system.** Even after a cell crosses that first threshold, it's not necessarily dangerous yet. Your immune system, specifically a type of cell called a natural killer (NK) cell, patrols the body looking for cells that are behaving abnormally. If it finds them, it kills them before they can become a problem. This happens constantly. Most of the time, you never know about it.

The immune system is a second, independent detection layer. You can fail Layer 1 and still be saved by Layer 2.

**Layer 3: The positional network.** Your cells are held in place by an adhesion system — molecular glue that keeps cells in their home tissue. Even a cancerous cell, as long as it's stuck where it belongs, can't spread. Metastasis — cancer spreading to other organs — requires a separate failure of this system. That's why the paper argues that carcinogenesis (cancer starting) and metastasis (cancer spreading) are two distinct events separated in time. Treating the primary tumor doesn't automatically address the spread risk. They have different failure mechanisms.

---

## The loop nobody was watching

Here's the part that was missing from the standard picture.

The immune system doesn't operate in isolation. It's connected to your brain, your stress response, and your nervous system — and those connections run in both directions.

**Tumor → Brain:** When a tumor grows, it produces inflammatory signals that eventually reach the brain. Those signals activate an enzyme called IDO, which hijacks the production of serotonin — redirecting the raw material toward a different pathway that produces a compound toxic to brain cells. This is one of the reasons cancer patients frequently experience depression, cognitive impairment, and severe fatigue. It's not a side effect of the psychological stress of having cancer. It's a direct biological cascade from the tumor to the brain.

**Brain → Tumor:** The reverse loop is equally important. Chronic stress elevates cortisol — your stress hormone. Cortisol directly suppresses two of the most important immune cells that patrol for cancer: NK cells and cytotoxic T cells. A cancer patient under chronic psychological stress is operating with their immune surveillance partially switched off. And cortisol damages the part of the brain (the hippocampus) that normally acts as a brake on the stress response — so elevated cortisol weakens its own off-switch, creating a loop that can sustain itself.

**The nervous system in the tumor itself:** Solid tumors are directly innervated by sympathetic nerves — the same nerves that activate your fight-or-flight response. When those nerves fire, they release norepinephrine directly into the tumor. That signal tells tumor cells to grow blood vessels, become harder to kill, and survive longer when they break off and circulate in the blood. Stress hormones don't just suppress your immune system — they actively promote tumor progression through a completely separate pathway.

None of these loops are managed in standard oncology practice. They're not measured, not monitored, and not treated as primary endpoints. The paper argues they should be.

## Exercise is not a lifestyle recommendation

One of the clearest takeaways from mapping this system is what physical inactivity actually does.

Exercise supports immune surveillance through four parallel mechanisms simultaneously:

1. It mobilizes NK cells from the spleen and bone marrow into circulation — directly increasing the immune patrol available to find cancer.
2. It normalizes cortisol — reducing the stress hormone suppression of immune surveillance.
3. It activates a metabolic pathway (AMPK) that competes directly with the tumor's preferred fuel source, making the environment around the tumor less hospitable.
4. It strengthens vagal nerve tone — which activates a reflex that suppresses the inflammatory signals the tumor uses to hijack your brain chemistry.

All four of these channels are suppressed simultaneously by physical inactivity. No single drug currently activates all four at once.

This isn't "exercise is good for you." This is: physical inactivity is a mechanism by which immune surveillance fails, through four specific biological pathways that are individually documented and collectively unaddressed by standard treatment.

## What the paper proposes measuring

Standard oncology currently monitors two things: tumor size (imaging) and tumor markers in blood (CEA, PSA, etc.). Both of these measure the *output* of the failure — the tumor that has already formed and is growing.

The paper argues for measuring the *inputs* — the upstream parameters that determine whether the immune surveillance system is capable of doing its job. Specifically:

- **Kynurenine:tryptophan ratio** — tells you whether the IDO pathway is active, suppressing immune cells and driving the brain cascade
- **Cortisol diurnal slope** — tells you whether the stress hormone system is chronically elevated, suppressing NK cells and T cells
- **Heart rate variability (HRV)** — tells you the sympathetic/parasympathetic balance in real time, a proxy for how pro-tumorigenic the nervous system environment is
- **NK cell cytotoxicity** — not just how many NK cells you have, but whether they can actually kill — which drops earlier than cell count when the system is compromised

None of these require new technology. They require ordering existing tests across specialties that currently don't communicate with oncology.

---

## Seven predictions

The paper derives seven specific testable predictions from this framework — things that should be true if the model is correct, that haven't been formally tested yet. The most concrete ones:

- Patients with high stress markers (flat cortisol, low HRV) should have worse tumor immune infiltrate than patients with normal markers, even at the same disease stage
- Exercise before immunotherapy should improve response rates by warming up "cold" tumors that weren't triggering immune surveillance
- Measuring the IDO activation marker (K:T ratio) before giving IDO inhibitor drugs should rescue a drug class that failed in a Phase III trial — because the trial gave the drug to everyone regardless of whether IDO was actually the problem

---

## The short version

Cancer is a detection failure. Your body has multiple systems designed to catch it — molecular checkpoints inside cells, immune patrols in the bloodstream, a positional system that keeps cells from spreading. Cancer requires most of them to fail.

What this paper maps is everything that can suppress those systems from the outside: chronic stress, physical inactivity, poor sleep, the tumor's own signaling into the nervous system and brain. These aren't soft "lifestyle factors." They are specific biological mechanisms with identified molecular pathways that directly degrade the immune surveillance system's ability to do its job.

Standard oncology treats the tumor. This paper argues it should also treat the conditions that allowed the tumor to evade detection in the first place — and monitor whether those conditions have actually been corrected, not just whether the visible tumor has shrunk.
