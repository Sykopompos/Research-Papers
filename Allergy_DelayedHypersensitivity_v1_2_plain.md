# The Wrong Test at the Wrong Time
## A Plain-Language Summary

*Based on the preprint: "The Wrong Test at the Wrong Time: A Mechanistic Framework for Non-IgE and Delayed Hypersensitivity Diagnosis" — M. Finnegan, March 2026*

---

## The One-Sentence Version

When doctors test for allergies, they almost always run a test that measures one specific type of immune reaction — but a large number of food and drug reactions work through completely different biology, and the standard test is structurally incapable of finding them.

---

## The Problem: A System Built for One Type of Allergy

Most people have a picture of what an allergic reaction looks like: someone eats a peanut, their throat starts to close, they need an EpiPen. That reaction happens within minutes because a specific type of immune cell — a mast cell — fires like a grenade when it detects the allergen. The standard allergy test (skin prick test or blood IgE test) is designed specifically to detect that grenade mechanism.

That test is excellent at what it does. The problem is that doctors routinely use it for reactions that work completely differently — and when the test comes back negative, the patient is often told they don't have an allergy. In reality, they just don't have *that kind* of allergy.

The three conditions this paper focuses on are:

- **Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)** — a chronic swallowing disorder caused by a specific type of white blood cell (eosinophils) accumulating in the esophagus in response to food
- **FPIES** — a condition in infants (and increasingly adults) where the wrong food causes severe, repetitive vomiting 1–4 hours after eating
- **Delayed drug reactions** (DRESS, SJS) — severe reactions to medications that appear 2–6 weeks after the drug was started

For all three of these, the standard allergy test is negative. Not because there's no immune reaction — there very clearly is — but because the immune pathway doesn't use the molecule the test measures.

---

## Why Reactions Are Delayed: The Slow Build

The reason delayed reactions are delayed is actually simple once you understand the mechanism. Think of it like this:

**Immediate allergic reaction (minutes):** The immune cell has already loaded and armed a grenade. When it sees the trigger, it fires immediately. This is what the standard allergy test detects.

**Delayed reaction (hours to days):** There's no preloaded grenade. Instead, when the trigger gets through a damaged protective barrier in the gut or esophagus, the immune system starts slowly recruiting an army — calling in more and more inflammatory cells over hours. By the time enough cells have arrived to cause noticeable symptoms, it's been 24–96 hours since the original trigger. The patient had the problem food 2–4 days ago and has no idea it's connected.

This is why **food diaries fail** for these conditions. If you write down what you ate when you felt sick, you're recording the wrong meal by 2–4 days.

---

## The Alarm System Behind It All

The paper describes a biological alarm system that links all these conditions together. It works like this:

1. The lining of the gut, esophagus, or skin gets damaged — sometimes by genetics, sometimes by acid reflux, sometimes by detergents or infections
2. The damaged cells release three alarm signals: **TSLP, IL-33, and IL-25** (think of these as emergency flares)
3. These flares activate a type of immune cell called an **ILC2** (group 2 innate lymphoid cell) — a first-responder that doesn't need to have seen the specific trigger before
4. The ILC2s release chemicals (IL-5, IL-13) that recruit eosinophils and cause inflammation
5. Crucially: **none of this requires the molecule the allergy test measures**

This is why IgE testing is negative in EoE and FPIES. The alarm system is triggered directly by barrier damage and IL-13 signals, not by the IgE pathway the test looks for.

## What Tests Actually Work

The paper lays out what each test actually measures and what it's actually good for:

| Condition | Standard test used | What actually works |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| EoE (food triggers) | IgE blood test / skin prick (negative in most patients) | 6-week milk elimination diet + endoscopy to confirm; work up to 4-food elimination if needed |
| FPIES | IgE blood test (negative) | Clinical pattern recognition; supervised food challenge |
| Delayed drug reactions (DRESS/SJS) | Allergy testing (useless) | HLA genetic testing *before* prescribing high-risk drugs |
| Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (lung) | IgE testing (useless) | Serum precipitins (a different antibody test that measures IgG) |

The most exciting near-term advance is a blood marker called **eotaxin-3**. In EoE, this marker starts rising when the esophagus is being triggered — potentially days before symptoms appear and before eosinophil counts reach the diagnostic threshold. A simple blood test during food reintroduction might one day replace or reduce the need for repeated endoscopies. The cutoff values haven't been validated yet, but this is the highest priority for non-invasive EoE monitoring.

## The Nervous System Layer (Still Being Worked Out)

The paper includes a section on how the nervous system amplifies allergic reactions. This is supported by molecular research but hasn't been directly measured in EoE or FPIES patients yet, so it's flagged as a hypothesis rather than established fact.

The short version: a chemical called **Substance P** (SP) is released from pain-sensing nerves when tissues are stressed. It directly triggers mast cells independently of the allergy pathway — which is why stress lowers allergic thresholds, why getting sick lowers your tolerance for trigger foods, and why exercise can sometimes trigger reactions that wouldn't happen at rest.

There's also a protective signal called **VIP** from a different type of nerve in the airway. In asthma patients, this signal is depleted — which is why their airways become hyperreactive and their immune cells in the airway are more easily triggered.

**Estradiol** (the main female sex hormone) primes mast cells to fire more easily. This explains why women have higher rates of atopic disease (asthma, eczema, rhinitis) after puberty than men — not before puberty, but after. It also explains why asthma worsens premenstrually in many women. Note: EoE itself is slightly more common in men, which is consistent with EoE being driven by a different mechanism (the barrier/ILC2 pathway) rather than the mast cell pathway.

## What the Paper Predicts

The paper makes 7 concrete predictions that could be tested:

1. **Blood eotaxin-3 at days 7–14 of food reintroduction predicts whether that food is causing EoE** — before symptoms appear and before endoscopy is needed. This is the most immediately actionable prediction.

2. **EoE patients who are IgE-positive to their trigger food will have functionally inactive IgE** (detectable by basophil activation test) — confirming the IgE is coincidental, not causal.

3. **An anti-nausea drug (aprepitant) should reduce EoE symptoms** without changing the eosinophil count — because it blocks the nervous system amplification pathway, not the immune pathway.

4. **FPIES patients should show specific protein surges in the blood** (TNF-α and IL-8) at 4–8 hours after eating the trigger food — which could become the first blood-based diagnostic test for FPIES.

5. **EoE symptoms should worsen in the high-estrogen phase of the menstrual cycle** — not because eosinophils increase, but because sensory nerve thresholds change with hormonal phase.

6. **A targeted elimination diet guided by molecular allergy testing (CRD)** should achieve the same remission rate as empiric 6-food elimination, with fewer foods eliminated.

7. **A VIP analogue inhaled before exercise** should reduce mast cell activation (measured by blood tryptase) more than a standard inhaler — because it restores the missing protective signal, not just the bronchodilation.

## Why the 5–7 Year Diagnosis Delay for EoE

EoE patients wait an average of 5–7 years to be diagnosed. The paper argues this isn't because EoE is rare or mysterious — it's because the test that patients are given first (IgE allergy panel) is structurally incapable of detecting it. A negative result gets filed as "no allergy," and the patient continues experiencing swallowing problems, food impactions, and chronic discomfort while the cause is missed.

The diagnosis requires an endoscopy with biopsy — counting eosinophils in a tissue sample. No blood test currently replaces that. But the framework in this paper identifies eotaxin-3 as the most plausible near-term non-invasive marker, and proposes a specific study design (P1 above) to validate it.

## The Drug Reaction Angle

The drug hypersensitivity sections are perhaps the clearest example of the paper's thesis. DRESS (Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms) is a severe, potentially fatal drug reaction that appears 2–6 weeks after starting a medication. It has nothing to do with the immediate allergy pathway. Allergy testing is irrelevant — not unhelpful, literally irrelevant, because the mechanism doesn't use the pathway allergy tests measure.

What does work: a genetic test before prescribing. Certain HLA gene variants predict with high accuracy which patients will have a severe reaction to abacavir (HIV medication) or carbamazepine (anti-seizure drug). This test is now standard of care for these drugs. HLA-B*57:01 testing for abacavir has essentially eliminated abacavir-induced hypersensitivity in populations where testing is implemented.

This is the cleanest example of the paper's core principle: for delayed immune reactions, the correct diagnostic strategy targets the upstream detection architecture — identify who will react before exposure — not the downstream inflammation after the reaction has already happened.

---

## The Bottom Line

The diagnostic mismatch this paper describes is not a failure of individual doctors. It's a structural problem: the only widely available test for "allergy" was designed for one specific mechanism, and clinical practice over-generalised it to all immune reactions. For patients with EoE, FPIES, delayed drug reactions, and hypersensitivity pneumonitis, the standard test answers a question that isn't being asked.

The paper doesn't claim these conditions are the same as each other — they have distinct mechanisms, different cells involved, different timescales, and different treatments. What unifies them is structural: they all operate through pathways the standard test ignores, and they all benefit from a mechanism-matched diagnostic approach that asks *which immune pathway is actually involved* before choosing which test to run.
