# The Major Non-Alzheimer's Dementias: Plain Language Summary

*A companion to the technical paper "The Major Non-Alzheimer's Dementias: Vascular, Lewy Body, and Frontotemporal Dementia" (v1.5, March 2026)*

*M. Finnegan · Preprint · Not yet peer reviewed*

---

## What this paper is about

Most people have heard of Alzheimer's. Far fewer know that roughly 30–40% of dementia cases are caused by something entirely different — three separate diseases with distinct mechanisms, distinct warning signs, distinct genetics, and distinct treatment needs. This paper covers those three:

- **Vascular dementia (VaD)** — caused by damage to the brain's blood supply
- **Lewy body disease (DLB)** — caused by a misfolded protein spreading through the brain
- **Frontotemporal dementia (FTD)** — caused by genetic failures in brain cells' internal machinery

Understanding them matters because the treatments that work for Alzheimer's often don't work for these conditions — and in the case of Lewy body disease, some common drugs are actively dangerous.

---

## Vascular Dementia: When the Brain's Blood Supply Fails

**What it is:** The brain needs constant, precise blood flow. When the small blood vessels supplying the brain's white matter are damaged — by high blood pressure, diabetes, atherosclerosis — patches of the brain are chronically under-supplied, eventually dying. These "white matter hyperintensities" accumulate silently over years, and when enough have built up, cognitive decline follows.

**How it's different from Alzheimer's:** Vascular dementia tends to affect speed of thinking and executive function (planning, switching between tasks) more than memory. Decline can be stepwise rather than gradual — a mini-stroke causes a noticeable step down, then stability, then another step.

**The key biology:** The brain's blood vessels are lined with endothelial cells that produce nitric oxide (NO) to keep vessels relaxed and the blood-brain barrier intact. High blood pressure and diabetes damage this system — the endothelial cells start producing damaging free radicals instead of protective NO, the barrier breaks down, and the vessels stiffen. The protective cells around brain capillaries (pericytes) are among the first casualties.

**What can be done:** Vascular dementia is the most preventable dementia. Aggressive blood pressure control (the SPRINT MIND trial showed intensive BP control reduced cognitive decline), treating diabetes, stopping smoking, and managing atrial fibrillation all directly reduce vascular brain damage. A 2023 trial (LACI-2) established the safety of a combination drug approach targeting the damaged vessel biology directly — the first mechanism-targeted therapy beyond blood pressure control.

**Biomarkers:** White matter lesions on MRI are the primary marker. A 2024 blood test measuring a protein called PlGF (placental growth factor) may detect vascular damage to the brain before MRI changes appear — potentially the first early warning blood test for this condition.

## Lewy Body Disease: When a Protein Spreads Through the Brain

**What it is:** Lewy body disease (which includes both "dementia with Lewy bodies" and Parkinson's disease dementia) is caused by the misfolding and spreading of a protein called alpha-synuclein. Once misfolded, it spreads from cell to cell like an infection, following anatomical pathways from the gut and brainstem upward through the brain.

**The gut-first hypothesis:** One of the more striking ideas in this paper is that Lewy body disease may begin in the gut — specifically in the enteric nervous system (the "second brain" in your digestive tract) — and spread to the brain via the vagus nerve years to decades before any brain symptoms appear.

**REM sleep behavior disorder as an early warning:** People with Lewy body disease often develop a condition called RBD (REM sleep behavior disorder) — they act out their dreams, sometimes violently, because the normal muscle paralysis during dreaming fails. About 75% of people with confirmed RBD will develop a Lewy body disease within 10 years, rising to about 90% by 14 years. A confirmed RBD diagnosis is now recognized as one of the earliest detectable signs of early Lewy body pathology, potentially decades before cognitive symptoms.

**The locus coeruleus connection:** The same small brainstem region (locus coeruleus) that drives the brain's waste clearance system is one of the first brain areas where Lewy body pathology appears. This means the disease impairs the brain's waste disposal at exactly the point it most needs it. The norepinephrine-producing neurons there also regulate brain inflammation, sleep, and memory formation — so their early damage creates multiple simultaneous downstream failures.

**Why it's dangerous to treat like Alzheimer's:** Patients with Lewy body disease have a potentially life-threatening reaction to antipsychotic medications (neuroleptic sensitivity) that are routinely used in dementia care. About 50% of patients experience severe, sometimes fatal reactions including sudden cognitive collapse, rigidity, and prolonged loss of consciousness. This is not a mild side effect — it's a medical emergency. Carers and clinicians need to know this before any psychiatric medication is prescribed.

**Diagnosis:** A new blood/spinal fluid test (alpha-synuclein SAA) can detect misfolded alpha-synuclein with approximately 95% accuracy. However, only about 68% of people *clinically diagnosed* with Lewy body disease test positive — meaning about a third of clinical diagnoses are catching something else. This is actually good news for precision: it means trials and treatments can now be targeted to patients who biologically have the disease.

**Treatments:** No disease-modifying therapy is approved. A recent Phase 2b trial of neflamapimod (RewinDLB) failed its primary endpoint in December 2024. The most promising directions are gene therapy targeting GBA1 (the strongest genetic risk factor) and atomoxetine (a norepinephrine drug) for the locus coeruleus depletion. The field is roughly a decade behind Alzheimer's therapeutics.

## Frontotemporal Dementia: When Brain Cells' Internal Machinery Fails

**What it is:** FTD is the most common dementia in people under 60. Unlike Alzheimer's (which primarily affects memory) or vascular dementia (which affects speed and executive function), FTD typically begins with personality and behavioral changes — impulsivity, loss of empathy, socially inappropriate behavior — or with language deterioration. Memory is often intact in the early stages.

**Why it's fundamentally different:** Alzheimer's and vascular dementia both involve the brain's ability to clear or protect against damage from outside the cell. FTD is primarily an inside-the-cell failure. The proteins that go wrong (TDP-43, tau, or FUS) are part of the cell's own RNA processing machinery. This means the Alzheimer's drugs that clear amyloid or protect blood vessels are essentially irrelevant to FTD.

**Strong genetic component:** About 30–40% of FTD cases run in families, with mutations in three main genes:
- **C9orf72** — the most common cause of both familial FTD and familial ALS (motor neuron disease)
- **GRN** — causes FTD by halving the production of a protein (progranulin) the brain needs to clear damaged proteins
- **MAPT** — causes FTD by throwing the normal balance of tau protein isoforms off

**Major 2024–2025 advances:**

*HDGFL2 biomarker:* Researchers discovered that when TDP-43 (the protein that misfolds in ~45% of FTD) stops working properly, it leaves a distinctive signature in spinal fluid and blood — a piece of a protein called cryptic HDGFL2. This was detected in people who were genetically destined to develop FTD but showed no symptoms yet, making it the first potential blood test for pre-symptomatic FTD of this type. Large-scale validation is still needed.

*KCNQ2 mechanism:* A 2025 study found that TDP-43 malfunction disrupts the production of a potassium channel (KCNQ2) that normally prevents neurons from over-firing. Without it, neurons become hyperactive. This explains a long-observed feature of ALS/FTD that was previously unexplained, and points toward a new class of drug target. Currently only demonstrated in lab-grown neurons — not yet tested in animals or humans.

**Treatment pipeline:**

*Latozinemab (INFRONT-3 trial, October 2025):* The first major Phase 3 trial for FTD — testing a drug that raises progranulin levels in GRN-mutation patients — failed its primary cognitive endpoint. Progranulin levels were successfully raised; cognitive benefit was not achieved. This raises hard questions about whether treatment needs to start much earlier (before significant neurodegeneration), whether progranulin is the right target, or whether the cognitive measurement tool was sensitive enough.

*PBFT02 gene therapy:* A different approach — delivering a working copy of the GRN gene directly into the spinal fluid — is showing more promising results. In mid-2025 interim data, treated patients had dramatically elevated progranulin levels maintained through 18 months, with much slower increase in a neurodegeneration blood marker (NfL) compared to untreated patients. Three patients had serious adverse events (blood clots), prompting protocol changes. A pivotal trial is being designed for 2026.

*TPN-101:* A repurposed HIV drug that works on a different mechanism (blocking LINE-1 reverse transcriptase, which is implicated in inflammatory signaling) showed positive signals in a Phase 2 trial in C9orf72-related ALS/FTD and has been selected for a major ALS platform trial.

## How the three diseases compare

| | Vascular | Lewy Body | Frontotemporal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **First symptom usually** | Slowed thinking, balance issues | REM sleep disorder, mild motor changes | Personality change or language loss |
| **Memory early?** | Variable | Often relatively preserved early | Often preserved early |
| **Age of onset** | 70s+ | 60s–70s | 45–65 (younger than other dementias) |
| **Main cause** | Blood vessel damage | Alpha-synuclein protein spreading | Genetic cell-machinery failure |
| **Preventable?** | Substantially (blood pressure, lifestyle) | Partially (lifestyle factors help) | Mostly genetic — limited prevention |
| **Key danger** | Silent accumulation before symptoms | Dangerous reaction to antipsychotics | Often misdiagnosed as psychiatric disorder |

## The mixed pathology problem

After age 75, most people with dementia don't have just one of these diseases — they have combinations. The most common is Alzheimer's plus vascular damage. The second most common is Alzheimer's plus Lewy body pathology. This matters because:

1. Single-disease trials may miss patients who have two simultaneous processes
2. Treating one component while ignoring the other produces limited benefit
3. The biological staging advances (being able to test which proteins are actually present) are beginning to make precision diagnosis possible for the first time

The paper argues that mixed pathology is not the exception in older patients — it is the rule, and clinical and research approaches need to account for it.

---

## Where the field is headed

All three conditions are approximately 10–20 years behind Alzheimer's in therapeutic development. The most important recent shifts are:

- **Biological staging** is arriving for Lewy body disease (NSD-ISS framework) and beginning for FTD — moving from diagnosing by symptoms to diagnosing by biology
- **Blood tests** are improving rapidly and will eventually allow population-level screening before symptoms
- **The 68% problem** in Lewy body disease (only 68% of clinical diagnoses are biologically confirmed) will drive precision trial design going forward

The practical message for patients and families: these are distinct diseases that require different approaches, and the correct biological diagnosis matters — for safety (especially in Lewy body disease), for accessing the right clinical trials, and for understanding the prognosis.
