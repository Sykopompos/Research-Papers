# Type 2 Diabetes: What's Actually Going On
### A Plain-Language Summary of *"Insulin Signaling Gate Failure, Ectopic Lipid Accumulation, and the Dual Threshold Structure of Metabolic Disease"*

*M. Finnegan — March 2026 · Full paper: github.com/Sykopompos/Research-papers*

---

## The One-Sentence Version

Type 2 diabetes is a fat storage problem that happens to show up as a blood sugar problem — and once you understand that, almost everything else about the disease makes sense.

---

## The Problem Isn't Your Pancreas. Not At First.

Most people think of diabetes as a problem with insulin production — your pancreas fails, you don't make enough insulin, blood sugar goes up. That's Type 1. Your immune system destroys the cells that make insulin.

Type 2 is completely different. In Type 2, your pancreas is working fine — often *too* hard. The problem is that the *doors* that insulin is supposed to open have been jammed shut.

Here's how it works:

Insulin's job is to act like a key. It shows up at your muscle cells and unlocks a door (called GLUT4) so that sugar from your blood can get inside. Your muscles burn the sugar, blood sugar goes down, everything works.

In Type 2 diabetes, the lock has been gummed up. Insulin shows up, tries to turn the lock, and nothing happens. Blood sugar stays high. Your pancreas notices and makes *more* insulin to try harder. Blood sugar still stays high. Eventually your pancreas burns out from the overwork. That's when blood sugar gets really out of control.

What's jamming the lock? Fat. Specifically, fat in places it shouldn't be.

---

## The Real Problem: Fat Overflowing Into the Wrong Places

Your body has a system for storing fat. It's supposed to go into fat cells — subcutaneous fat, the stuff you can pinch under your skin. That fat is mostly harmless. It's the body doing its job.

The problem starts when fat cells get full. When they can't store any more, fat starts spilling over into places it was never meant to be: your liver, your muscles, and your pancreas. This is called **ectopic lipid accumulation** — "ectopic" just means "in the wrong place."

This overflow fat releases chemicals (called DAG and ceramide) that physically block the insulin signalling system. The lock gets jammed. Insulin keeps knocking but the door doesn't open.

**This is why the number on the scale is misleading.** The relevant question isn't how much fat you have — it's where the fat is. Here's the proof: in studies where surgeons removed about 10 kilograms (22 lbs) of fat through liposuction, the patients' blood sugar, insulin sensitivity, cholesterol, and blood pressure didn't improve at all. Because they removed subcutaneous fat — the safe kind. The metabolic danger comes from the fat in and around your organs, not the fat under your skin.

Waist circumference is actually a better predictor of Type 2 diabetes than BMI for exactly this reason.

---

## Three Places the Fat Does Damage

**In your muscles:** Fat overflow blocks the insulin key from working. Insulin shows up, but GLUT4 doors stay closed. Blood sugar can't get in.

**In your liver:** Fat causes a weird paradox. Normally insulin tells the liver to do two things: stop making glucose, and don't make too much fat. In a fatty liver, one of those instructions gets blocked but the other doesn't. The liver stops listening to "stop making glucose" (so you get high blood sugar, especially in the morning) but keeps making fat (so you get high triglycerides). Same disease, producing both problems simultaneously.

**In your pancreas:** Fat gradually kills the cells that make insulin. They burn out, get toxic, and die. This part is partially irreversible — once enough cells are gone, they don't come back. This is why catching the disease early matters so much.

---

## Your Brain Is Involved Too

The brain controls hunger and how much you eat. In particular, there's a region of the brain called the hypothalamus that acts like a thermostat for your energy balance — it receives signals from your gut, your fat, your hormones, and uses them to decide whether you're hungry.

In Type 2 diabetes, this thermostat gets broken in two ways:

**The insulin signal stops working in the brain** (same mechanism as in the muscles). The brain can no longer accurately read how much energy you have, so it keeps telling you to eat even when you've had enough.

**The leptin signal stops working too.** Leptin is a hormone made by fat cells that tells your brain "you have enough energy stored, stop eating." In people with a lot of visceral fat, the brain stops responding to leptin — so the signal that should turn off hunger gets ignored.

This is a biological failure of the hunger regulation system. It's not a lack of willpower. The sensors are broken.

### The Reward System Also Gets Hijacked

There's a separate system in the brain that handles food reward — the pleasure of eating. In people who regularly eat highly palatable foods (high fat, high sugar), this system gets desensitised, the same way it does with addictive substances. The bar for feeling satisfied gets raised. More food is needed to feel the same reward.

This is one reason that obesity can be self-reinforcing — and why drugs like semaglutide and tirzepatide work in ways that go beyond just reducing appetite. They actually reset these brain circuits.

## Hormones Make It Worse

**Women going through menopause** face a double hit. Estrogen normally helps muscle cells respond to insulin and keeps fat in the safer subcutaneous areas. When estrogen drops at menopause, insulin sensitivity drops too, and fat shifts to the visceral (dangerous) pattern. This is why women's risk of Type 2 diabetes increases substantially after menopause — it's a hormonal mechanism, not just ageing.

**PCOS** (polycystic ovary syndrome) creates a vicious cycle: insulin resistance → more insulin in the blood → ovaries produce more testosterone → testosterone makes insulin resistance worse → even more insulin. Once it starts, each step makes the next step worse. The correct treatment targets the insulin resistance, not the testosterone directly.

**Testosterone in men** works like estrogen in women — it helps maintain muscle mass and keeps fat in safer places. Low testosterone in men independently increases diabetes risk.

**Stress and cortisol** directly raise blood sugar and promote fat accumulation around the organs. Chronic stress doesn't just affect your mood — it's feeding the ectopic lipid problem directly.

---

## Your Sleep Schedule Is Part of This

This is one of the less widely known parts of the story.

Every cell in your body runs on a roughly 24-hour clock. Your pancreas has its own internal clock that times when insulin gets released. When that clock gets disrupted — by shift work, irregular sleep, or sleep apnea — the pancreas loses its timing. Specifically, it loses "first-phase secretion" — the rapid early burst of insulin that normally occurs right after a meal. Without this, blood sugar spikes higher and stays higher.

**Sleep deprivation** directly raises the hunger hormone ghrelin and lowers the fullness hormone leptin — making you hungrier even when you've eaten enough. It also activates the stress hormone cortisol. These effects happen after just a few nights of poor sleep, independent of any weight gain.

**Sleep apnea** — where breathing repeatedly stops during sleep — causes nightly spikes in cortisol and free fatty acids. Over 85% of obese people with Type 2 diabetes also have sleep apnea, most of them undiagnosed. Each apnea episode is essentially triggering a mini stress response that floods the body with the same chemicals that cause insulin resistance.

## The Disease Is More Reversible Than You've Been Told

A major clinical trial (DiRECT) showed that 46% of people with Type 2 diabetes went into complete remission — no diabetes at all — through dietary weight management. Not just "better controlled." Gone. A five-year follow-up showed some people maintained this.

The reason: **the ectopic fat that was causing the problem can be removed.** When you lose fat from your liver and pancreas (not just from under your skin), the insulin lock starts working again. Liver fat can normalise within about a week of aggressive caloric restriction — it's the fastest-responding part of the body. Pancreatic fat takes longer.

**The catch:** this works best early. Once enough of the insulin-producing cells have died and the body has made certain permanent changes (called "metabolic memory") to how it manages inflammation, the disease becomes harder to reverse. This is why the prediabetes stage — before any cells have died — is the optimal window to intervene.

## How the Drugs Actually Work

Most diabetes drugs treat the blood sugar (the symptom), not the overflow fat (the cause). That's why they work, but often don't achieve remission.

**Metformin** is the exception among traditional drugs — it helps the liver stop overproducing glucose. It's not perfect but it addresses something more upstream than just lowering blood sugar.

**GLP-1 drugs (semaglutide, liraglutide / Ozempic, Victoza)** work on the brain and the pancreas simultaneously. They reset the hunger thermostat, reduce the reward drive for food, improve pancreatic insulin release, and — by causing weight loss — reduce the ectopic fat that's causing the problem in the first place. Major trials showed they also dramatically reduce heart attacks, strokes, and kidney failure.

**Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound)** does everything GLP-1 drugs do, plus adds a second signal through a related system (GIP). It achieves even greater weight loss and appears to have additional metabolic benefits. In a three-year trial in people with prediabetes, tirzepatide reduced the progression to full diabetes by 94%.

**SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin / Jardiance, Farxiga)** work completely differently — they make the kidneys dump excess sugar into the urine. They don't fix the underlying problem but they bypass it, and they have proven benefits for heart failure and kidney disease that appear independent of blood sugar lowering.

**Bariatric surgery** is the most powerful intervention — people often see their diabetes resolve before they've lost much weight, because the surgery triggers large hormonal changes that reset multiple systems at once.

## It's Connected to Other Diseases

The same ectopic fat and insulin signalling problems that drive Type 2 diabetes also appear in other conditions:

**Heart disease:** T2D multiplies cardiovascular risk 2–4 fold through arterial damage caused by chronic high blood sugar and inflammation. The same cortisol/stress pathway connects stress → insulin resistance → heart disease.

**Alzheimer's disease:** The brain uses insulin signalling too. Hippocampal insulin resistance may impair memory formation through similar mechanisms to muscle insulin resistance. Post-menopausal women with T2D face disproportionately elevated Alzheimer's risk — losing estrogen's protective effects at the same time insulin resistance is increasing.

**Stroke:** High blood sugar at the time of a stroke makes brain damage significantly worse.

These connections are why the paper argues that "metabolic syndrome" — the cluster of obesity, high blood pressure, high triglycerides, low HDL, and high blood sugar — isn't five separate problems. It's one problem (ectopic lipid overflow) showing up in five places at once.

## Six Experiments That Would Confirm or Break This

The paper proposes six specific, testable predictions. Here are two of the most interesting:

**Prediction 1:** If the beta-cell clock disruption idea is right, shift workers should specifically lose the *first* burst of insulin after eating (the fast early spike), while their overall insulin production stays relatively normal. Cross-sectional study comparing shift workers to day workers with identical body weights and HbA1c levels would test this directly.

**Prediction 3:** If sleep apnea is causing insulin resistance through nightly fat and cortisol spikes, then treating apnea with a CPAP machine should specifically restore that early insulin burst — independent of any weight change. An RCT comparing CPAP to sham-CPAP over six months with insulin secretion as the primary endpoint would answer this.

---

## The Bottom Line

Type 2 diabetes is a fat storage overflow problem. When fat overflows into your liver, muscles, and pancreas, it physically blocks insulin from doing its job. The brain's hunger regulation breaks down. Hormonal systems add to the problem. Sleep disruption compounds it. Blood sugar going up is the symptom that gets measured — it's the last step in a chain that started years earlier.

The good news: the underlying problem is partially reversible, especially early. The overflow fat can be reduced. The insulin locks can be unclogged. The brain circuits can be reset. The disease is not inevitable, and in many cases it can be sent into remission.

The bad news: the current medical system mostly treats the blood sugar (the symptom) rather than the overflow fat (the cause). And the disease is usually detected about a decade after the underlying problem began — by which point some damage is permanent.

The earlier you intervene, the more is reversible.
